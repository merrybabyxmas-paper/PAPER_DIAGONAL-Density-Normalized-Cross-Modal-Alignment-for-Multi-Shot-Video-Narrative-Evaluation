\begin{abstract}
Text-to-multi-shot video (T2MSV) generation models are evaluated primarily on visual quality and frame-level consistency, yet these metrics fail to capture whether models can execute \emph{narrative dynamics}---the structured entry and exit of entities across shots.
We formalize multi-shot narratives as \emph{state transition matrices} $\Delta S \in \{-1,0,+1\}^{(T-1) \times N}$, where each element encodes an atomic operation: entry ($+1$), exit ($-1$), or hold ($0$).
We prove that all possible 3-shot narrative topologies can be spanned by just two basis matrices under column permutation and linear combination (\textbf{Theorem~1}), establishing mathematical completeness.
Building on this, we introduce \diagonal{}, a benchmark comprising 1,000 prompts spanning 7 narrative patterns (3 basis, 4 derived) with complementary metrics: Narrative Alignment Score, Diagonal Dominance, State Transition Accuracy, Entity Presence Accuracy, and SVD Entropy.
Evaluation of four SOTA T2MSV architectures reveals that \emph{no model achieves positive diagonal dominance on any pattern} ($\epsilon$-pass $= 0\%$), exposing a fundamental limitation we term \textbf{Narrative Blindness}.
We identify four systematic failure types---Context Bleeding (VIC: 60.8\%), Amnesia (VGoT: 66.7\%), Attention Reversion, and Identity Bleeding---and validate via human evaluation ($\rho = 0.80$ with STA).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ============================================================

Diffusion-based generative models have rapidly expanded the capabilities of video synthesis, enabling a transition from single-clip generation to text-to-multi-shot video (T2MSV) generation.
Recent systems such as StoryDiffusion~\cite{storydiff}, EchoShot~\cite{echoshot}, Video-In-Context (VIC)~\cite{vic}, and VGoT~\cite{vgot} can generate temporally connected sequences of scenes from textual descriptions, suggesting that multi-shot video generation is approaching practical usability.

However, the apparent success of these systems is largely driven by evaluation protocols that overlook a fundamental aspect of narrative structure.
Existing benchmarks~\cite{vbench,vbench2,t2vcompbench,moviebench} evaluate identity consistency, visual quality, or single-clip compositionality, but none systematically test whether models can execute the \emph{narrative dynamics} that define cinematic storytelling: the structured introduction, co-occurrence, and removal of entities across shots.

Consider a simple relay narrative: $A \to AB \to B$, where entity $A$ appears alone, both $A$ and $B$ co-occur, then only $B$ remains.
This requires three capabilities: (1)~introducing $B$ while preserving $A$, (2)~maintaining co-occurrence fidelity, and (3)~removing $A$ while $B$ persists.
As we demonstrate, current models systematically fail at capability~(3)---the departed entity ``bleeds'' into subsequent shots (\textbf{Context Bleeding}).
Critically, existing holistic metrics like Identity Consistency (IC)~\cite{shotadapter} \emph{reward} this failure because persistent entities increase cross-shot similarity.
We term this the \textbf{consistency paradox}: IC is adversarial to narrative compliance for any non-trivial pattern.

We formalize entity dynamics as a \emph{state transition matrix} $\Delta S \in \{-1,0,+1\}^{2 \times 3}$ and prove that \textbf{all} 3-shot topologies are generated from two basis matrices via permutation and linear combination (\textbf{Theorem~1}).
Building on this, \diagonal{} contributes:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
  \item A topological formalization with a completeness guarantee for multi-shot narrative structures (\S\ref{sec:formalization}).
  \item A \textbf{1,000-prompt benchmark} spanning 7 patterns with decomposed metrics---NAS, DD, STA, EPA, SVD Entropy---that isolate different aspects of narrative compliance (\S\ref{sec:metrics}).
  \item We expose the \textbf{consistency paradox}: holistic similarity metrics systematically reward narrative failures (\S\ref{sec:blindspot}).
  \item Evaluation of four SOTA T2MSV architectures revealing universal \textbf{Narrative Blindness}, a four-type failure taxonomy, and human evaluation validation (\S\ref{sec:experiments}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%% ============================================================

\paragraph{Multi-shot video generation.}
Diffusion-based video synthesis has advanced from UNet temporal attention~\cite{svd,imagenvideo} through cascaded pipelines~\cite{ho2022video,makeavideo} to DiT architectures~\cite{dit,wan}.
Multi-shot extensions address narrative-level coherence via diverse mechanisms: shared self-attention~\cite{storydiff}, cross-attention conditioning~\cite{videostudio}, latent initialization~\cite{mevg}, LLM-guided layouts~\cite{vgot,videodirectorgpt}, keyframe transitions~\cite{seine}, flow-matching portraits~\cite{echoshot}, and in-context LoRA~\cite{vic}.
These span four paradigms: (1)~Consistent Self-Attention (StoryDiffusion), (2)~flow-matching (EchoShot), (3)~in-context DiT (VIC), and (4)~cascaded T2I+I2V (VGoT).
We evaluate representatives from each paradigm.

\paragraph{Video generation benchmarks.}
Existing benchmarks quantify consistency via embedding similarity (Table~\ref{tab:benchmark}).
VBench 2.0~\cite{vbench,vbench2} computes frame-level similarities that conflate subject and background; T2V-CompBench~\cite{t2vcompbench} targets single-clip compositionality without multi-shot transitions.
ShotAdapter~\cite{shotadapter} isolates subject regions via IC, but was designed for single-entity shots---applying IC to multi-entity scenes produces a \emph{chimera embedding} that conflates co-occurring entities, rendering entity-removal failures invisible.
MovieBench~\cite{moviebench} and LoCoT2V-Bench~\cite{locot2v} recognize multi-shot challenges but lack entity-level automated metrics and mathematical formalization.
Video-Bench~\cite{videobench} targets video understanding rather than generation.
To our knowledge, \diagonal{} is the first benchmark to formalize multi-shot narratives via state transition matrices and provide topological completeness guarantees.

\section{The Consistency Paradox}
\label{sec:blindspot}
%% ============================================================

Before introducing our framework, we demonstrate a structural flaw in existing evaluation: holistic similarity metrics systematically \emph{reward} narrative failures.

ShotAdapter's Identity Consistency (IC)~\cite{shotadapter} computes mean pairwise CLIP similarity across all shots.
When a narrative requires entity changes (e.g., $A \to AB \to B$), a model that correctly removes entity $A$ will produce \emph{lower} cross-shot similarity than one that incorrectly preserves $A$ in all shots.
IC thus penalizes correct narrative execution.

Formally, let $\|\Delta S\|_F > 0$.
The IC-maximizing generation has $\Delta S_{\text{gen}} = \mathbf{0}$---the static sequence ignoring all dynamics.
We validate empirically: across 3,049 generated videos, IC and STA are negatively correlated ($r = -0.34$).
VIC achieves the highest IC but the \emph{lowest} STA (0.431), precisely because its in-context conditioning preserves all entities regardless of instructions.
StoryDiffusion---highest STA (0.630)---records lower IC because successful entity removals reduce cross-shot similarity.

This finding has profound implications: prior work reporting ``improved identity consistency'' may be inadvertently selecting for models that are \emph{worse} at narrative dynamics.

\section{Topological Formalization}
\label{sec:formalization}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{State Transition Matrix}

The temporal evolution of entities across shots is encoded as:
\begin{equation}
\Delta S \in \mathbb{M}_{(T-1) \times N}\!\bigl(\{-1, 0, +1\}\bigr),
\label{eq:delta_s}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta S_{t,e}$ records the state change of entity $e$ from shot $t$ to $t\!+\!1$: entry ($+1$), exit ($-1$), or hold ($0$).
We focus on $T\!=\!3, N\!\leq\!3$, yielding a $2 \times 3$ matrix capturing the minimal complete narrative cycle.

\subsection{Basis and Derived Patterns}

Three \emph{basis patterns} represent fundamental archetypes:

\textbf{Relay} ($\calB_1$: $A \!\to\! AB \!\to\! B$). Narrative handoff---$B$ enters while $A$ exits:
$\calB_1 = \bigl[\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & +1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr] = E_{12} - E_{21}$.

\textbf{Split} ($\calB_2$: $AB \!\to\! A \!\to\! B$). Co-occurring entities separate:
$\calB_2 = \bigl[\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ -1 & +1 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr] = -E_{12} - E_{21} + E_{22}$.

\textbf{Accumulation} ($\calB_3$: $A \!\to\! AB \!\to\! ABC$). Progressive introduction:
$\calB_3 = \bigl[\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & +1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & +1 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr] = E_{12} + E_{23}$.

Four \emph{derived patterns} are obtained via time-reversal $\calT$ (negate and reverse rows) and column permutation $\calP_{ij}$:
\textbf{Reduction} ($ABC \!\to\! AB \!\to\! A$) $= \calT(\calB_3)$;
\textbf{Convergence} ($A \!\to\! B \!\to\! AB$) $= \calP_{AB}(\calT(\calB_2))$;
\textbf{Reverse Relay} ($B \!\to\! AB \!\to\! A$) $= \calP_{AB}(\calB_1)$;
\textbf{Sliding Window} ($AB \!\to\! BC \!\to\! C$) $=$ superposition of relay and exit.

\subsection{Completeness Theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Completeness of the Narrative Space]
Under column permutation $\calP_{ij}$ and integer-coefficient linear combination, $\calB_1$ (Relay) and $\calB_2$ (Split) span $\mathbb{M}_{2 \times 3}(\mathbb{Z})$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We construct all six standard basis matrices $E_{ij}$ from $\calB_1$ and $\calB_2$.

\textbf{Step~1.}
Define $v_1 = \calB_1 = E_{12} - E_{21}$,
$v_2 = \calP_{13}(\calB_1) = E_{12} - E_{23}$,
$v_3 = \calP_{23}(\calP_{12}(\calB_1)) = E_{11} - E_{23}$.
Then $X = v_3 - v_2 + v_1 = E_{11} - E_{21}$.

\textbf{Step~2.}
$X + \calP_{12}(\calB_2) = (E_{11} - E_{21}) + (-E_{11} + E_{21} - E_{22}) = -E_{22}$.

\textbf{Step~3.}
From $E_{22}$, permutations yield $E_{21}, E_{23}$.
From $X + E_{21}$: $E_{11}$.
Further permutations: $E_{12}, E_{13}$.
All six $\{E_{ij}\}$ obtained; any $\Delta S \in \mathbb{M}_{2 \times 3}(\mathbb{Z})$ is their integer combination.
\end{proof}

\textbf{Implication.}
Theorem~1 guarantees that \diagonal{} is \emph{mathematically complete}: any conceivable 3-shot narrative topology is expressible as a linear combination of our basis patterns.
This distinguishes our benchmark from prior work that selects narrative types heuristically.

\section{The DIAGONAL Evaluation Framework}
\label{sec:metrics}
%% ============================================================

We define complementary metrics that decompose multi-shot narrative alignment into interpretable components.

\subsection{Cross-Modal Similarity Matrix}

For a generated video with 3 shots and evaluation prompts $\{T_1, T_2, T_3\}$:
\begin{equation}
\Mobs[i,j] = \cos\!\bigl(\Phi_V(V_i),\; \Phi_T(T_j)\bigr),
\label{eq:mobs}
\end{equation}
where $\Phi_V$ and $\Phi_T$ are CLIP ViT-L/14 visual and text encoders.
Each shot $V_i$ is represented by the mean-pooled feature of 5 uniformly sampled frames.

\subsection{Narrative Alignment Score (NAS)}
Row-normalized alignment measuring per-shot prompt specificity:
\begin{equation}
\text{NAS} = \frac{1}{3} \sum_{i=1}^{3} \frac{\Mobs[i,i]}{\sum_{j=1}^{3} \Mobs[i,j]}.
\label{eq:nas}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Diagonal Dominance (DD)}
Whether the correct prompt achieves highest similarity:
\begin{equation}
\text{DD}_i = \Mobs[i,i] - \max_{j \neq i} \Mobs[i,j].
\label{eq:dd}
\end{equation}
The $\epsilon$-\emph{pass rate} requires DD$_i > 0.02$ for all $i$.
DD $> 0$ means each shot aligns best with its intended prompt; DD $< 0$ indicates systematic misalignment.

\subsection{Entity Presence Accuracy (EPA)}
Per-entity CLIP probes: $E[i,k] = \cos(\Phi_V(V_i), \Phi_T(\text{``a } e_k\text{''}))$.
Binarized via per-shot median threshold:
\begin{equation}
\text{EPA} = \frac{1}{3N}\sum_{i,k} \mathbb{1}\!\left[\hat{P}_{ik} = P^*_{ik}\right].
\label{eq:epa}
\end{equation}

\subsection{State Transition Accuracy (STA)}
Fraction of atomic operations correctly reflected:
\begin{equation}
\text{STA} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{A}|} \sum_{\substack{(t,e) \in \mathcal{A}}} \mathbb{1}\!\left[\text{sgn}(\Delta E_{t,e}) = \text{sgn}(\Delta S_{t,e})\right],
\label{eq:sta}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{A} = \{(t,e) : \Delta S_{t,e} \neq 0\}$ and $\Delta E_{t,e} = E[t\!+\!1,e] - E[t,e]$.
STA isolates the most challenging aspect: correctly executing state transitions.

\subsection{SVD Entropy}
Per-shot semantic complexity from frame-feature SVD:
\begin{equation}
\calH(V_t) = -\sum_i \tilde{\sigma}_i \log \tilde{\sigma}_i, \quad \tilde{\sigma}_i = \sigma_i / {\textstyle\sum_j} \sigma_j.
\label{eq:svd_entropy}
\end{equation}
The \emph{trajectory correctness rate} measures whether $\Delta\calH$ matches entity count changes.

\subsection{Failure Taxonomy}
Four types based on discrepancies between $\Delta S$ and observed changes:

\textbf{Type~I: Context Bleeding} ($\Delta S\!=\!-1$, entity persists).
The departed entity's visual signature remains in subsequent shots.

\textbf{Type~II: Amnesia} ($\Delta S\!=\!+1$, entity absent).
A new entity fails to appear despite explicit prompt instruction.

\textbf{Type~III: Attention Reversion.}
The diagonal shows a U-shape ($M_{11} > M_{22} < M_{33}$), indicating reversion to initial content.

\textbf{Type~IV: Identity Bleeding.}
Near-uniform similarity rows indicate failure to distinguish entities visually.

\section{Dataset Construction}
\label{sec:dataset}
%% ============================================================

We construct 1,000 multi-shot scenarios (3,000 shots) spanning 7 narrative patterns derived from our topological formalization (\S\ref{sec:formalization}).

Basis patterns receive 200 prompts each; derived patterns receive 100 each (Table~\ref{tab:pattern_dist}), reflecting the algebraic structure where derived patterns are compositions of basis patterns.

\paragraph{Prompt design.}
Prompts are programmatically generated via Google Gemini with structured templates specifying:
(1)~narrative pattern and entity assignments,
(2)~core entities drawn from 20 categories (human, animal, professional),
(3)~scene themes from 10 environments, and
(4)~per-shot textual descriptions embedding entity dynamics naturally.
Each prompt has three variants: \texttt{gen\_prompt} (with quality tags), \texttt{eval\_prompt} (clean description for CLIP evaluation), and \texttt{diag\_prompt} (entity-focused probe for presence detection).
Entity pairs are selected for visual distinguishability (mean CLIP text similarity: 0.23, $\sigma = 0.08$).

\section{Experiments}

\subsection{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Models.}
We evaluate four T2MSV architectures spanning major design paradigms:
\textbf{StoryDiffusion}~\cite{storydiff} (CSA~\cite{sdxl} + SEINE~\cite{seine} I2V, 30+50 steps),
\textbf{EchoShot}~\cite{echoshot} (flow-matching, Wan2.1-1.3B~\cite{wan}, 50 steps),
\textbf{VIC}~\cite{vic} (CogVideoX-5B~\cite{cogvideox} + LoRA, 50 steps), and
\textbf{VGoT}~\cite{vgot} (Kolors T2I + DynamiCrafter~\cite{dynamicrafter} I2V, 25+50 steps).
All use seed = 42.
VIC completed 449/1000 and VGoT 600/1000 scenarios due to generation failures; we report on successfully generated videos.

\paragraph{Evaluation.}
Five uniformly-spaced frames per shot, processed by CLIP ViT-L/14~\cite{clip}.
All experiments on 4 NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPUs.
Threshold details in supplementary.

The in-context mechanism explicitly references prior shots, making entity removal particularly difficult.
\textbf{VGoT's Amnesia (66.7\%)} exposes a planning-rendering bottleneck: the LLM structures narratives, but DynamiCrafter cannot generate new entities on demand.
\textbf{StoryDiffusion's Identity Bleeding (21.7\%)} stems from CSA promoting visual homogeneity across subjects.
Only 9.1--23.2\% of videos are free from all failure types.

Types I and II co-occur in 31.2\% of videos, indicating systemic inability to manage entity transitions rather than isolated failures.
Types III and IV show low mutual co-occurrence (2.1\%), suggesting distinct architectural deficiencies (detailed co-occurrence analysis in supplementary).

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ============================================================

We have presented \diagonal{}, a topologically grounded evaluation framework for multi-shot video generation that formalizes narrative dynamics as state transition matrices.
Our key theoretical contribution---proving that two basis patterns span the complete narrative space (Theorem~1)---provides the first mathematical completeness guarantee for a video generation benchmark.

Evaluation of four SOTA architectures reveals universal \textbf{Narrative Blindness}: no model achieves positive diagonal dominance on any pattern, and 77--91\% of generated videos exhibit at least one systematic failure type.
The failure taxonomy identifies architecture-specific vulnerabilities: VIC suffers from Context Bleeding (60.8\%) due to its in-context conditioning, VGoT from Amnesia (66.7\%) due to planning-rendering decoupling, and StoryDiffusion from Identity Bleeding (21.7\%) due to CSA homogenization.
We further expose the \emph{consistency paradox}---holistic metrics reward narrative failures---validated analytically ($r = -0.34$ between IC and STA) and through human evaluation ($\rho = 0.80$ with STA).

Our results suggest that progress requires fundamentally new approaches to compositional entity control---mechanisms that can independently manage entity introduction, persistence, and removal within a unified generation framework, rather than relying on holistic conditioning that conflates narrative structure with visual consistency.
Potential directions include entity-specific attention masks, narrative-conditioned classifier-free guidance, and entity slot architectures that decouple identity from presence.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
\diagonal{} currently evaluates 3-shot sequences with up to 3 entities; extension to variable-length narratives with branching entity timelines is a natural next step.
Our CLIP-based metrics capture coarse-grained entity presence; combining with detection-based methods (e.g., GroundingDINO~\cite{groundingdino}) could improve granularity.
VIC (449/1000) and VGoT (600/1000) did not complete all scenarios; results should be interpreted with this caveat.
We release our benchmark, evaluation code, and all generated videos to facilitate reproducible research.
