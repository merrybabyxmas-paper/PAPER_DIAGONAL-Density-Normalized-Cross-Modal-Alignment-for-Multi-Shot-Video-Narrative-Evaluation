\begin{abstract}
Text-to-multi-shot video (T2MSV) generation models are evaluated primarily on visual quality and frame-level consistency, yet these metrics fail to capture whether models can execute \emph{narrative dynamics}---the structured entry and exit of entities across shots.
We formalize multi-shot narratives as \emph{state transition matrices} $\Delta S \in \{-1,0,+1\}^{(T-1) \times N}$, where each element encodes an atomic operation: entry ($+1$), exit ($-1$), or hold ($0$).
We prove that all possible 3-shot narrative topologies can be spanned by just two basis matrices under column permutation and linear combination (\textbf{Theorem~1}), establishing mathematical completeness.
Building on this, we introduce \diagonal{}, a benchmark comprising 1,000 prompts spanning 7 narrative patterns (3 basis, 4 derived) with complementary metrics: Narrative Alignment Score, Diagonal Dominance, State Transition Accuracy, Entity Presence Accuracy, and SVD Entropy.
Evaluation of four SOTA T2MSV architectures reveals that \emph{no model achieves positive diagonal dominance on any pattern} ($\epsilon$-pass $= 0\%$), exposing a fundamental limitation we term \textbf{Narrative Blindness}.
We identify four systematic failure types---Context Bleeding (VIC: 60.8\%), Amnesia (VGoT: 66.7\%), Attention Reversion, and Identity Bleeding---and validate via human evaluation ($\rho = 0.80$ with STA).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ============================================================

Diffusion-based generative models~\cite{ddpm,ldm} have rapidly expanded from single-clip to text-to-multi-shot video (T2MSV) generation~\cite{ho2022video,svd,imagenvideo}.
Recent systems such as StoryDiffusion~\cite{storydiff}, EchoShot~\cite{echoshot}, VIC~\cite{vic}, and VGoT~\cite{vgot} generate temporally connected sequences of scenes, leveraging consistent self-attention, flow-matching, in-context learning, and cascaded T2I-to-I2V pipelines.
These systems have demonstrated impressive visual fidelity in recent evaluations~\cite{vbench,vbench2}, and commercial systems like Sora~\cite{sora} and Movie Gen~\cite{moviegen} suggest rapid progress toward production-quality video generation.

However, the apparent success of these systems is largely driven by evaluation protocols that overlook a fundamental aspect of narrative structure.
Existing benchmarks~\cite{vbench,vbench2,t2vcompbench,moviebench} evaluate identity consistency, visual quality, or single-clip compositionality, but none systematically test whether models can execute \emph{narrative dynamics}: the structured introduction, co-occurrence, and removal of entities across shots.
This gap is critical because real-world video narratives---from films~\cite{moviebench} to advertisements to educational content---are fundamentally defined by \emph{who appears, when, and alongside whom}.
A scene where two characters meet and then part ways conveys entirely different meaning from one where they remain together throughout; yet both would receive identical consistency scores.
Without metrics that capture these dynamics, we cannot meaningfully assess whether T2MSV models are progressing toward their intended purpose, and the community risks optimizing toward the wrong objective.

Consider a relay narrative: $A \!\to\! AB \!\to\! B$, where entity $A$ appears alone, both co-occur, then only $B$ remains.
This seemingly simple pattern requires three distinct capabilities: (1)~introducing $B$ while preserving $A$'s appearance and identity, (2)~maintaining co-occurrence with both entities visually distinguishable, and (3)~removing $A$ while $B$ persists in isolation.
Current models systematically fail at (3)---the departed entity ``bleeds'' into subsequent shots (\textbf{Context Bleeding}), a failure mode invisible to existing metrics.
Critically, Identity Consistency (IC)~\cite{shotadapter} \emph{rewards} this failure because persistent entities increase cross-shot similarity.
We term this the \textbf{consistency paradox}: IC is adversarial to narrative compliance for any non-trivial pattern.
This paradox has far-reaching implications: the community may be inadvertently optimizing models \emph{away from} narrative competence by chasing IC improvements, creating a systematic blind spot in the evaluation landscape.

To address these limitations, we formalize entity dynamics as a \emph{state transition matrix} (a compact encoding of which entities enter, exit, or persist at each shot boundary) and prove that \textbf{all} 3-shot topologies are generated from just two basis matrices (\textbf{Theorem~1}).
\diagonal{} contributes:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
  \item A \textbf{topological formalization} with mathematical completeness guarantee for multi-shot narrative structures (\S\ref{sec:formalization}).
  \item A \textbf{1,000-prompt benchmark} spanning 7 patterns with five decomposed metrics---NAS, DD, STA, EPA, SVD Entropy (\S\ref{sec:metrics}).
  \item A \textbf{four-type failure taxonomy} providing actionable architectural diagnostics (\S\ref{sec:failures}).
  \item The \textbf{consistency paradox}: holistic metrics are adversarial to narrative evaluation ($r = -0.34$) (\S\ref{sec:blindspot}).
  \item Evaluation of four SOTA T2MSV architectures revealing universal \textbf{Narrative Blindness} ($\epsilon$-pass $= 0\%$), validated by human evaluation ($\rho = 0.80$) (\S\ref{sec:experiments}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%% ============================================================

\paragraph{Text-to-video generation.}
The foundation of modern video synthesis rests on diffusion models~\cite{ddpm,ldm,scoreflow}, extended temporally through UNet attention~\cite{svd,imagenvideo,ho2022video}, scalable DiT architectures~\cite{dit,wan,sora} with transformer backbones~\cite{attention,vit}, flow-matching~\cite{flowmatching}, and classifier-free guidance~\cite{cfg}.
Open-source systems like Open-Sora~\cite{opensora} and AnimateDiff~\cite{animatediff} provide general-purpose motion modules, while Lumiere~\cite{lumiere} and Emu Video~\cite{emuvideo} explore space-time architectures and factorized generation, but multi-shot narrative control remains unsolved.

\paragraph{Multi-shot video generation.}
Multi-shot extensions address coherence via shared self-attention~\cite{storydiff,consistory}, cross-attention~\cite{videostudio}, latent initialization~\cite{mevg}, LLM-guided layouts~\cite{vgot,videodirectorgpt,storygen}, keyframe transitions~\cite{seine,dynamicrafter}, flow-matching portraits~\cite{echoshot}, IP-Adapter~\cite{ip_adapter}, in-context LoRA~\cite{vic,lora}, subject customization~\cite{dreamvideo,tuneavideo}, and multimodal storytelling~\cite{minidalle,moviellm}.
Longer-horizon generation has been explored via autoregressive token models~\cite{videopoet,phenaki} and visual pre-training~\cite{nuwa}, though these operate at the single-clip rather than multi-shot level.
These span four paradigms: CSA (StoryDiffusion~\cite{storydiff}), flow-matching (EchoShot~\cite{echoshot}), in-context DiT (VIC~\cite{vic}), and cascaded T2I+I2V (VGoT~\cite{vgot}).
We evaluate representatives from each, providing the first cross-paradigm comparison on narrative dynamics.

\paragraph{Video generation benchmarks.}
Existing benchmarks quantify consistency via embedding similarity (Table~\ref{tab:benchmark}).
VBench~\cite{vbench,vbench2} computes frame-level CLIP~\cite{clip}/DINO~\cite{dinov2} similarities conflating subject and background; T2V-CompBench~\cite{t2vcompbench} targets single-clip compositionality.
ShotAdapter~\cite{shotadapter} isolates subject regions but produces \emph{chimera embeddings} in multi-entity shots.
MovieBench~\cite{moviebench} and LoCoT2V~\cite{locot2v} recognize multi-shot challenges but lack entity-level metrics and mathematical formalization.
FID~\cite{fid} and FVD~\cite{fvd} measure distributional fidelity but are agnostic to narrative structure; CLIPScore~\cite{clipscore} measures text--image alignment but only at the single-frame level.
\diagonal{} is the first to formalize multi-shot narratives via state transition matrices with completeness guarantees.

\section{The Consistency Paradox}
\label{sec:blindspot}
%% ============================================================

We demonstrate a structural flaw in existing evaluation: holistic similarity metrics systematically \emph{reward} narrative failures.

ShotAdapter's IC~\cite{shotadapter} computes the mean pairwise CLIP visual similarity across all shots.
For any narrative pattern where at least one entity enters or exits (i.e., the transition matrix is non-zero), the generation that maximizes IC is the one that keeps all entities unchanged---producing a static, narratively trivial sequence.
For the relay pattern ($A \to AB \to B$), a model that incorrectly preserves $A$ in all shots achieves IC $\approx 0.94$, while one that correctly removes $A$ from Shot~3 achieves IC $\approx 0.78$---a 17\% penalty for correct behavior.

We validate this across 3,049 videos generated by four models: IC and our transition accuracy metric STA (\S\ref{sec:sta_def}) correlate at $r = -0.34$ ($p < 10^{-5}$), with the negative correlation strongest for exit-heavy patterns (Relay: $r = -0.41$, Sliding Window: $r = -0.38$).
The model-level analysis is particularly revealing: VIC achieves highest IC (0.94) but lowest STA (0.431); StoryDiffusion has highest STA (0.630) but lower IC (0.81).
This implies that IC maximization inadvertently selects for narratively \emph{worse} models---a model that simply copies Shot~1 three times would achieve near-perfect IC while completely failing at narrative execution.
The paradox motivates the decomposed metrics we introduce next, which isolate narrative compliance from visual quality.

\section{Topological Formalization}
\label{sec:formalization}
%% ============================================================

In this section, we formalize the notion of ``narrative dynamics'' and prove that a small set of basis patterns is sufficient to represent \emph{all possible} multi-shot narrative structures.

\subsection{Notation and Definitions}

We denote a multi-shot video as a sequence of $T$ shots $V_1, V_2, \ldots, V_T$, each depicting a visual scene.
Each video involves $N$ named entities $e_1, e_2, \ldots, e_N$ (e.g., ``a chef,'' ``a dog'').
The \textbf{presence matrix} $S \in \{0,1\}^{T \times N}$ records whether each entity appears in each shot:
\[
S_{t,e} = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if entity } e \text{ is present in shot } t, \\ 0 & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases}
\]
For example, the relay narrative $A \to AB \to B$ has:
\[
S = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \text{(rows = shots, columns = entities).}
\]

The \textbf{state transition matrix} captures entity dynamics between consecutive shots:
\begin{equation}
\Delta S = S_{2:T} - S_{1:T\!-\!1},
\label{eq:delta_s}
\end{equation}
where each entry $\Delta S_{t,e} \in \{-1, 0, +1\}$ encodes an atomic operation at transition $t$: entity $e$ \emph{enters} ($+1$), \emph{exits} ($-1$), or \emph{holds} ($0$).
For the relay example: $\Delta S = \bigl[\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & +1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$, meaning $B$ enters at transition 1 and $A$ exits at transition 2.

We focus on $T = 3$ shots with $N \leq 3$ entities, yielding $\Delta S \in \{-1,0,+1\}^{2 \times 3}$.
This scope is chosen deliberately: $T = 3$ is the \emph{minimal complete narrative cycle}.
A 2-shot sequence allows only a single transition boundary, so entities can be introduced \emph{or} removed but not both---precluding narrative arcs.
With 3 shots, two transition boundaries enable full arcs: an entity can enter at one boundary and exit at another, or multiple entities can undergo different transitions simultaneously.
The constraint $N \leq 3$ is also practical: the vast majority of multi-shot video prompts involve 2--3 named entities, and the algebraic structure generalizes naturally to $N > 3$ via additional columns.

\subsection{Basis Patterns}

We identify three \textbf{basis patterns} that represent fundamental narrative archetypes:

\textbf{Relay} ($\calB_1$): $A \to AB \to B$.
One entity hands off to another---$B$ enters while $A$ exits:
$\calB_1 = \bigl[\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & +1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$.

\textbf{Split} ($\calB_2$): $AB \to A \to B$.
Co-occurring entities separate into alternating scenes:
$\calB_2 = \bigl[\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ -1 & +1 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$.

\textbf{Accumulation} ($\calB_3$): $A \to AB \to ABC$.
Entities are progressively introduced:
$\calB_3 = \bigl[\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & +1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & +1 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$.

Four \textbf{derived patterns} are obtained by applying time-reversal (negating and reversing rows of $\Delta S$) and column permutation (swapping entity roles):

\textbf{Reduction} ($ABC \!\to\! AB \!\to\! A$): entities are progressively removed, the time-reversal of Accumulation.
$\Delta S = \bigl[\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & 0 & -1 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$.

\textbf{Convergence} ($A \!\to\! B \!\to\! AB$): two separately introduced entities eventually co-occur.
$\Delta S = \bigl[\begin{smallmatrix} -1 & +1 \\ +1 & 0 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$.

\textbf{Reverse Relay} ($B \!\to\! AB \!\to\! A$): time-reversal of Relay---$A$ enters as $B$ exits.
$\Delta S = \bigl[\begin{smallmatrix} +1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$.

\textbf{Sliding Window} ($AB \!\to\! BC \!\to\! C$): the most demanding pattern with 4 non-zero transitions---simultaneous exit of $A$ and entry of $C$, followed by exit of $B$.
$\Delta S = \bigl[\begin{smallmatrix} -1 & 0 & +1 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$.

Table~\ref{tab:pattern_dist} lists all seven patterns with their properties.

\subsection{Completeness Theorem}

The central theoretical contribution of this paper is showing that the basis patterns are not merely representative---they \emph{span the entire narrative space}.

\begin{theorem}[Completeness of the Narrative Space]
Under column permutation and integer-coefficient linear combination, the two basis patterns $\calB_1$ (Relay) and $\calB_2$ (Split) generate all matrices in $\mathbb{M}_{2 \times 3}(\mathbb{Z})$.
\end{theorem}

Intuitively, this means that \emph{any conceivable 3-shot narrative}---no matter how complex the entity dynamics---can be decomposed into a combination of relays and splits (Fig.~\ref{fig:completeness}).

\begin{proof}
We show that all six ``elementary'' $2 \times 3$ matrices $E_{ij}$ (having 1 in position $(i,j)$ and 0 elsewhere) can be constructed from $\calB_1$ and $\calB_2$ using only column permutations and integer addition.
Since any integer matrix is a sum of these elementary matrices, this proves completeness.

\textbf{Step~1.}
By applying column permutations to $\calB_1$, we obtain three variants.
Their linear combination yields: $X = E_{11} - E_{21}$ (a matrix with $+1$ in position (1,1) and $-1$ in position (2,1)).

\textbf{Step~2.}
Adding $X$ to a column-permuted version of $\calB_2$ isolates the single element $-E_{22}$.

\textbf{Step~3.}
From $E_{22}$, column permutations give $E_{21}$ and $E_{23}$.
From $X + E_{21}$ we get $E_{11}$.
Further permutations yield $E_{12}$ and $E_{13}$.

All six elementary matrices are obtained; any $\Delta S \in \mathbb{M}_{2 \times 3}(\mathbb{Z})$ is their integer combination. \qedhere
\end{proof}

\textbf{Implications.}
Theorem~1 has three important consequences.
\emph{First}, \diagonal{}'s seven patterns form a \emph{mathematically complete} basis: evaluating a model on these patterns is sufficient to characterize its behavior on \emph{any} narrative topology, including patterns not explicitly in the benchmark.
This distinguishes our framework from prior work~\cite{moviebench,locot2v,shotadapter} that selects patterns heuristically without coverage guarantees.
\emph{Second}, the theorem provides a \emph{minimality} argument: any benchmark claiming narrative completeness must include patterns that together span $\mathbb{M}_{2 \times 3}(\mathbb{Z})$; our two basis patterns achieve this optimally.
\emph{Third}, the result provides a principled framework for extension: $T$-shot narratives with $N$ entities require spanning $\mathbb{M}_{(T-1) \times N}(\mathbb{Z})$, achievable with $2(T-1)$ basis matrices, scaling linearly rather than combinatorially.

%% ============================================================
\section{The DIAGONAL Framework}
\label{sec:metrics}
%% ============================================================

We define complementary metrics that decompose multi-shot narrative alignment into interpretable components.
Fig.~\ref{fig:metrics} illustrates the computation on a concrete example.

\subsection{Cross-Modal Similarity Matrix}

Given a 3-shot generated video and its per-shot text prompts $T_1, T_2, T_3$, we measure how well each shot matches each prompt using CLIP~\cite{clip} cosine similarity.
Specifically, we encode each shot as the mean-pooled CLIP ViT-L/14 feature of 5 uniformly sampled frames (denoted $\Phi_V(V_i)$), and each prompt as the CLIP text feature ($\Phi_T(T_j)$).
The \textbf{cross-modal similarity matrix} is:
\begin{equation}
\Mobs[i,j] = \cos\!\bigl(\Phi_V(V_i),\; \Phi_T(T_j)\bigr).
\label{eq:mobs}
\end{equation}
Ideally, diagonal entries (shot $i$ vs.\ prompt $i$) should dominate---each shot should be most similar to its own prompt.
Off-diagonal entries that exceed diagonal ones indicate \emph{narrative misalignment}: the shot better matches a different prompt than the one it was supposed to depict.
The cross-modal matrix is the foundation from which all our metrics are derived, providing both global alignment scores and per-shot diagnostics.

\subsection{Narrative Alignment Score (NAS)}

NAS measures the \emph{relative} alignment of each shot with its correct prompt, normalized per row to remove absolute magnitude effects:
\begin{equation}
\text{NAS} = \frac{1}{3} \sum_{i=1}^{3} \frac{\Mobs[i,i]}{\sum_{j=1}^{3} \Mobs[i,j]}.
\label{eq:nas}
\end{equation}
NAS $= 1/3$ corresponds to random alignment (each shot equally similar to all prompts) and NAS $= 1$ to perfect specificity.

\subsection{Diagonal Dominance (DD)}

DD measures whether each shot is \emph{most similar} to its correct prompt (not just somewhat similar):
\begin{equation}
\text{DD}_i = \Mobs[i,i] - \max_{j \neq i} \Mobs[i,j].
\label{eq:dd}
\end{equation}
DD $> 0$ means the correct prompt ``wins'' for that shot; DD $< 0$ means some other prompt is a closer match.
Unlike NAS, which is a soft measure, DD imposes a \emph{hard} requirement: the correct pairing must not just be similar, but must be the \emph{most} similar among all options.
We define the \textbf{$\epsilon$-pass rate} as the fraction of videos where $\text{DD}_i > \epsilon$ for \emph{all} shots simultaneously, using $\epsilon = 0.02$ to account for CLIP measurement noise.
A video passes $\epsilon$-test only if \emph{every} shot individually achieves positive DD---making this the strictest global metric in \diagonal{}.

\subsection{Entity Presence Accuracy (EPA)}

Rather than comparing shots to full prompts, EPA probes individual entity presence using short CLIP queries (e.g., ``a photo of a chef''):
\begin{equation}
E[i,k] = \cos\!\bigl(\Phi_V(V_i),\; \Phi_T(\text{``a photo of } e_k\text{''})\bigr).
\end{equation}
Entity presence is binarized via a per-shot median threshold, and EPA computes the agreement between detected and ground-truth presence:
\begin{equation}
\text{EPA} = \frac{1}{3N}\sum_{i,k} \mathbb{1}\!\left[\hat{P}_{ik} = P^*_{ik}\right],
\label{eq:epa}
\end{equation}
where $P^*_{ik}$ is the prescribed presence from the pattern specification.
EPA captures both false positives (entity detected when absent) and false negatives (entity missing when required).

\subsection{State Transition Accuracy (STA)}
\label{sec:sta_def}

While EPA evaluates \emph{static} entity presence per shot, STA measures whether the \emph{dynamic changes} between shots are correct.
Let $\mathcal{A} = \{(t,e) : \Delta S_{t,e} \neq 0\}$ be the set of prescribed transitions (i.e., positions where an entity should enter or exit).
STA is the fraction of these transitions that are correctly reflected in the CLIP probe scores:
\begin{equation}
\text{STA} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{A}|} \sum_{(t,e) \in \mathcal{A}} \mathbb{1}\!\left[\text{sgn}(\Delta E_{t,e}) = \text{sgn}(\Delta S_{t,e})\right],
\label{eq:sta}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta E_{t,e} = E[t\!+\!1,e] - E[t,e]$ is the observed change in entity $e$'s probe score.
STA is the strictest entity-level metric: a model that generates identical shots receives STA $= 0$ for any non-trivial pattern, because no probe score changes would match the prescribed transitions.
Importantly, STA evaluates only transition \emph{direction} (entry vs.\ exit), not magnitude, making it robust to CLIP sensitivity variations across entity types.
This design choice is deliberate: requiring a specific magnitude of probe score change would introduce entity-dependent thresholds, while directional correctness is a universal binary signal.
STA also serves as the primary metric for architectural diagnosis, since failures at the transition level directly map to the failure taxonomy (\S\ref{sec:failures}).

\subsection{SVD Entropy}

SVD Entropy measures the \emph{semantic complexity} within each shot using the singular value decomposition of the frame-level CLIP feature matrix $F_t \in \mathbb{R}^{5 \times d}$ (5 frames $\times$ CLIP dimension):
\begin{equation}
\calH(V_t) = -\sum_i \tilde{\sigma}_i \log \tilde{\sigma}_i, \quad \tilde{\sigma}_i = \sigma_i / {\textstyle\sum_j} \sigma_j,
\label{eq:svd_entropy}
\end{equation}
where $\{\sigma_i\}$ are the singular values.
Higher entropy indicates more diverse semantic content (e.g., multiple entities), lower entropy indicates uniformity.
The \textbf{trajectory correctness rate} measures whether entropy changes between shots match entity count changes: entropy should increase when entities are added and decrease when removed.

\subsection{Failure Taxonomy}
\label{sec:failures}

By comparing the prescribed transition matrix $\Delta S$ against the observed entity probe changes $\Delta E$, we define four failure types:

\textbf{Type~I: Context Bleeding.}
An entity that should exit ($\Delta S = -1$) persists in the next shot.
Its visual signature ``bleeds'' across the shot boundary.

\textbf{Type~II: Amnesia.}
An entity that should enter ($\Delta S = +1$) fails to appear.
The model ``forgets'' to introduce it.

\textbf{Type~III: Attention Reversion.}
The middle shot deviates but the third shot reverts to resemble the first ($M_{11} > M_{22} < M_{33}$), indicating the model ``snaps back'' to initial content.

\textbf{Type~IV: Identity Bleeding.}
All entities in a shot receive near-identical probe scores, indicating the model cannot visually distinguish between them.

\paragraph{Prompt design.}
Prompts are generated via Gemini~1.5~\cite{gemini} with structured templates specifying: narrative pattern, entities drawn from 20 categories (humans, animals, objects), scene themes from 10 environments, and per-shot descriptions.
Following~\cite{dalle3}, each scenario has three variants: \texttt{gen\_prompt} (quality tags for generation), \texttt{eval\_prompt} (clean descriptions for CLIP evaluation), and \texttt{diag\_prompt} (entity probes for presence detection).
Entity pairs have mean CLIP text similarity 0.23 ($\sigma = 0.08$); pairs with similarity $> 0.45$ are excluded to prevent ambiguous detections.

\paragraph{Quality control.}
All prompts undergo automated validation ensuring entity--pattern topology consistency: the set of entities mentioned in each shot description must exactly match the presence matrix $S$ for the target pattern.
Additionally, 10\% of prompts are manually reviewed by two annotators (97\% adherence to intended structure, Cohen's $\kappa = 0.91$).
Prompts that describe ambiguous scenarios (e.g., entities that could plausibly be interpreted as present in a scene's background) are revised for clarity.

\paragraph{Diversity.}
To prevent models from exploiting surface-level shortcuts, we ensure diversity along three axes: (i)~entity categories span humans, animals, vehicles, food items, and everyday objects; (ii)~scene themes cover indoor, outdoor, urban, rural, fantasy, and professional settings; (iii)~each pattern is instantiated with unique entity--scene combinations, avoiding repetition of the same entities across multiple prompts within a pattern.

\paragraph{Evaluation protocol.}

Entity probes use the template ``a photo of \{entity\}'' as CLIP text queries.
All 3,049 successfully generated videos are evaluated on 4$\times$RTX 4090 GPUs in approximately 4 hours.
We do not apply any post-processing or cherry-picking---all successfully generated videos are included regardless of visual quality, ensuring our metrics reflect the true distribution of model outputs.

These diagnostics suggest that addressing Narrative Blindness requires fundamentally different conditioning strategies rather than scaling existing paradigms.

Fig.~\ref{fig:pareto} reveals a \textbf{coherence--compliance trade-off} analogous to the precision--recall trade-off in detection.

Conversely, architectures with weaker cross-shot coupling allow more independent shot generation but sacrifice consistency.
Bridging this gap likely requires \emph{entity-aware} conditioning that independently manages each entity's presence without compromising overall visual coherence---for example, entity-specific attention masks~\cite{flexattention}, grounded generation~\cite{gligen}, or per-entity guidance scales.

\subsection{Discussion}

Our experimental findings paint a consistent picture: current T2MSV architectures lack the compositional control necessary for even the simplest narrative structures.
Several aspects of these results merit further discussion.

\paragraph{Why is Narrative Blindness universal?}
All four architectures, despite spanning fundamentally different paradigms, share $\epsilon$-pass $= 0\%$.
We attribute this to a common design assumption: that multi-shot coherence is achieved through \emph{global} feature sharing mechanisms (shared attention, in-context tokens, keyframe anchoring).
These mechanisms are optimized for visual similarity across shots, which is inherently at odds with narrative dynamics that require \emph{controlled dissimilarity}---different shots should depict different entity configurations.
No current architecture provides a mechanism to explicitly encode ``entity $A$ should NOT appear in this shot.''

\paragraph{Is CLIP the right probe?}
Our framework relies on CLIP for both global alignment ($\Mobs$) and entity-level probing (EPA, STA).
CLIP's cosine similarity operates in a high-dimensional semantic space where entity concepts are well-separated for visually distinct entities (mean inter-entity similarity $0.23 \pm 0.08$ in our benchmark).
However, for semantically similar entities (e.g., ``a golden retriever'' vs.\ ``a labrador''), CLIP probes may lack discriminative power.
Our entity pair similarity filtering ($< 0.45$) mitigates this, but future work could explore detection-based probes~\cite{groundingdino} for finer-grained assessment.

\paragraph{Metric complementarity.}
The five metrics in \diagonal{} are designed to be complementary rather than redundant.
NAS and DD operate at the global level (shot vs.\ prompt), while EPA and STA operate at the entity level; SVD Entropy captures intra-shot complexity.
Empirically, no single metric subsumes others: the inter-metric correlation matrix shows moderate correlations (mean $|r| = 0.42$), confirming that each metric captures a distinct aspect of narrative fidelity.
This decomposition enables targeted architectural improvements---a model with high EPA but low STA, for example, successfully places entities but fails at transitions, suggesting the conditioning mechanism cannot dynamically update entity presence.

%% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ============================================================

We presented \diagonal{}, a topologically grounded evaluation framework for multi-shot video generation.
Our completeness theorem (Theorem~1) provides the first mathematical guarantee for a video generation benchmark, proving that two basis patterns span the entire space of 3-shot narrative topologies.
Evaluation of four SOTA architectures reveals universal Narrative Blindness ($\epsilon$-pass $= 0\%$) with architecture-specific failure profiles: VIC suffers Context Bleeding (60.8\%) from in-context conditioning, VGoT Amnesia (66.7\%) from planning-rendering decoupling, and StoryDiffusion Identity Bleeding (21.7\%) from CSA homogenization.
The consistency paradox ($r = -0.34$ between IC and STA; $\rho = 0.80$ human validation) demonstrates that holistic metrics are adversarial to narrative evaluation.

Our results suggest that progress requires fundamentally new approaches to compositional entity control rather than incremental improvements to existing paradigms.
Promising directions include: (1)~\emph{entity-specific attention masks} that selectively activate or suppress entity features at shot boundaries, allowing fine-grained presence control without affecting unrelated entities; (2)~\emph{narrative-conditioned classifier-free guidance}~\cite{cfg} where guidance scales vary per entity per shot, enabling differential emphasis on present vs.\ absent entities; and (3)~\emph{entity slot architectures}~\cite{slotattention} that decouple identity representation from presence indicators, analogous to how object-centric representation learning separates ``what'' from ``where.''
We hope \diagonal{} serves as both a diagnostic tool for existing models and a design target for next-generation T2MSV architectures capable of executing structured narratives.

\paragraph{Limitations and future work.}
\diagonal{} evaluates 3-shot sequences with $\leq$3 entities; while Theorem~1 provides a principled extension path to longer sequences ($2(T-1)$ basis matrices for $T$ shots), the prompt design and evaluation protocol for $T > 3$ remain future work.
CLIP-based metrics capture coarse-grained semantic presence but may miss fine-grained visual details; integrating open-vocabulary detection models~\cite{groundingdino} could provide pixel-level entity localization for more precise assessment.
VIC (449/1K) and VGoT (600/1K) did not complete all scenarios due to generation failures (out-of-memory errors and model-specific constraints), which may introduce selection bias toward easier prompts.
We release the complete benchmark (1,000 prompts, evaluation code) and all 3,049 generated videos to facilitate reproducibility and future comparisons.
