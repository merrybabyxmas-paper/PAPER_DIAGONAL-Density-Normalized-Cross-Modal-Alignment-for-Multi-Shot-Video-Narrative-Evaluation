\begin{abstract}
Text-to-multi-shot video (T2MSV) generation models are evaluated primarily on visual quality and frame-level consistency, yet these metrics fail to capture whether models can execute \emph{narrative dynamics}---the structured entry and exit of entities across shots.
We formalize multi-shot narratives as \emph{state transition matrices} $\Delta S \in \{-1,0,+1\}^{(T-1) \times N}$, where each element encodes an atomic operation: entry ($+1$), exit ($-1$), or hold ($0$).
We prove that all possible 3-shot narrative topologies can be spanned by just two basis matrices under column permutation and linear combination (\textbf{Theorem~1}), establishing mathematical completeness.
Building on this, we introduce \diagonal{}, a benchmark comprising 1,000 prompts spanning 7 narrative patterns (3 basis, 4 derived).
A vision-language model constructs the observed presence matrix $S_\text{obs}$ via per-entity binary queries, yielding a clean matrix-matching pipeline: the \emph{Prescribed Transition Match} (PTM) measures the fraction of structurally prescribed transitions ($\Delta S \neq 0$ cells) correctly observed in the generated video.
Evaluation of four SOTA T2MSV architectures across 3,600 videos reveals statistically significant method rankings (Kruskal--Wallis $H = 275$, $p < 10^{-59}$): StoryDiffusion achieves the highest PTM (0.243) while VIC scores lowest (0.070), with all pairwise comparisons significant ($p < 0.005$) and non-overlapping bootstrap confidence intervals.
We identify a \emph{coherence--compliance Pareto dilemma} ($r_{\text{IC-PTM}} = -0.998$) and four systematic failure types, providing actionable architectural diagnostics.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ============================================================

Diffusion-based generative models~\cite{ddpm,ldm} have rapidly expanded from single-clip to text-to-multi-shot video (T2MSV) generation~\cite{ho2022video,svd,imagenvideo}.
Recent systems such as StoryDiffusion~\cite{storydiff}, EchoShot~\cite{echoshot}, VIC~\cite{vic}, and VGoT~\cite{vgot} generate temporally connected sequences of scenes, leveraging consistent self-attention, flow-matching, in-context learning, and cascaded T2I-to-I2V pipelines.
These systems have demonstrated impressive visual fidelity in recent evaluations~\cite{vbench,vbench2}, and commercial systems like Sora~\cite{sora} and Movie Gen~\cite{moviegen} suggest rapid progress toward production-quality video generation.

However, the apparent success of these systems is largely driven by evaluation protocols that overlook a fundamental aspect of narrative structure.
Existing benchmarks~\cite{vbench,vbench2,t2vcompbench,moviebench} evaluate identity consistency, visual quality, or single-clip compositionality, but none systematically test whether models can execute \emph{narrative dynamics}: the structured introduction, co-occurrence, and removal of entities across shots.
This gap is critical because real-world video narratives---from films~\cite{moviebench} to advertisements to educational content---are fundamentally defined by \emph{who appears, when, and alongside whom}.
A scene where two characters meet and then part ways conveys entirely different meaning from one where they remain together throughout; yet both would receive identical consistency scores.
Without metrics that capture these dynamics, we cannot meaningfully assess whether T2MSV models are progressing toward their intended purpose, and the community risks optimizing toward the wrong objective.

Consider a relay narrative: $A \!\to\! AB \!\to\! B$, where entity $A$ appears alone, both co-occur, then only $B$ remains.
This seemingly simple pattern requires three distinct capabilities: (1)~introducing $B$ while preserving $A$'s appearance and identity, (2)~maintaining co-occurrence with both entities visually distinguishable, and (3)~removing $A$ while $B$ persists in isolation.
Current models systematically fail at (3)---the departed entity ``bleeds'' into subsequent shots (\textbf{Context Bleeding}), a failure mode invisible to existing metrics.
Critically, Identity Consistency (IC)~\cite{shotadapter} \emph{rewards} this failure because persistent entities increase cross-shot similarity.
We term this the \textbf{consistency paradox}: IC is adversarial to narrative compliance for any non-trivial pattern.
This paradox has far-reaching implications: the community may be inadvertently optimizing models \emph{away from} narrative competence by chasing IC improvements, creating a systematic blind spot in the evaluation landscape.

To address these limitations, we formalize entity dynamics as a \emph{state transition matrix} (a compact encoding of which entities enter, exit, or persist at each shot boundary) and prove that \textbf{all} 3-shot topologies are generated from just two basis matrices (\textbf{Theorem~1}).
Our evaluation reveals a \emph{coherence--compliance Pareto dilemma}: no current architecture simultaneously achieves high visual coherence and high narrative compliance, suggesting a fundamental architectural tension that the community must resolve.
\diagonal{} contributes:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
  \item A \textbf{topological formalization} with mathematical completeness guarantee for multi-shot narrative structures (\S\ref{sec:completeness}).
  \item A \textbf{VLM-based matrix-matching pipeline} that constructs observed presence matrices via binary entity queries and evaluates them against prescribed transition matrices (\S\ref{sec:vlm_instantiation}).
  \item A \textbf{1,000-prompt benchmark} spanning 7 patterns with a primary metric (PTM) achieving statistically significant discrimination across all method pairs ($p < 0.005$) (\S\ref{sec:experiments}).
  \item A \textbf{four-type failure taxonomy} derived algebraically from the error matrix, providing actionable architectural diagnostics (\S\ref{sec:error_taxonomy}).
  \item The \textbf{consistency paradox}: holistic metrics are adversarial to narrative evaluation ($r_{\text{IC-PTM}} = -0.998$) (\S\ref{sec:blindspot}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%% ============================================================

\paragraph{Text-to-video generation.}
The foundation of modern video synthesis rests on diffusion models~\cite{ddpm,ldm,scoreflow}, extended temporally through UNet attention~\cite{svd,imagenvideo,ho2022video}, scalable DiT architectures~\cite{dit,wan,sora} with transformer backbones~\cite{attention,vit}, flow-matching~\cite{flowmatching}, and classifier-free guidance~\cite{cfg}.
Open-source systems like Open-Sora~\cite{opensora} and AnimateDiff~\cite{animatediff} provide general-purpose motion modules, while Lumiere~\cite{lumiere} and Emu Video~\cite{emuvideo} explore space-time architectures and factorized generation, but multi-shot narrative control remains unsolved.

\paragraph{Multi-shot video generation.}
Multi-shot extensions address coherence via shared self-attention~\cite{storydiff,consistory}, cross-attention~\cite{videostudio}, latent initialization~\cite{mevg}, LLM-guided layouts~\cite{vgot,videodirectorgpt,storygen}, keyframe transitions~\cite{seine,dynamicrafter}, flow-matching portraits~\cite{echoshot}, IP-Adapter~\cite{ip_adapter}, in-context LoRA~\cite{vic,lora}, subject customization~\cite{dreamvideo,tuneavideo}, and multimodal storytelling~\cite{minidalle,moviellm}.
Longer-horizon generation has been explored via autoregressive token models~\cite{videopoet,phenaki} and visual pre-training~\cite{nuwa}, though these operate at the single-clip rather than multi-shot level.
These span four paradigms: CSA (StoryDiffusion~\cite{storydiff}), flow-matching (EchoShot~\cite{echoshot}), in-context DiT (VIC~\cite{vic}), and cascaded T2I+I2V (VGoT~\cite{vgot}).
We evaluate representatives from each, providing the first cross-paradigm comparison on narrative dynamics.

\paragraph{Video generation benchmarks.}
Existing benchmarks quantify consistency via embedding similarity (Table~\ref{tab:benchmark}).
VBench~\cite{vbench,vbench2} computes frame-level CLIP~\cite{clip}/DINO~\cite{dinov2} similarities conflating subject and background; T2V-CompBench~\cite{t2vcompbench} targets single-clip compositionality.
ShotAdapter~\cite{shotadapter} isolates subject regions but produces \emph{chimera embeddings} in multi-entity shots.
MovieBench~\cite{moviebench} and LoCoT2V~\cite{locot2v} recognize multi-shot challenges but lack entity-level metrics and mathematical formalization.
FID~\cite{fid} and FVD~\cite{fvd} measure distributional fidelity but are agnostic to narrative structure; CLIPScore~\cite{clipscore} measures text--image alignment but only at the single-frame level.
\diagonal{} is the first to formalize multi-shot narratives via state transition matrices with completeness guarantees.

\section{The Consistency Paradox}
\label{sec:blindspot}
%% ============================================================

We demonstrate a structural flaw in existing evaluation: holistic similarity metrics systematically \emph{reward} narrative failures.

ShotAdapter's IC~\cite{shotadapter} computes the mean pairwise CLIP visual similarity across all shots.
For any narrative pattern where at least one entity enters or exits (i.e., the transition matrix is non-zero), the generation that maximizes IC is the one that keeps all entities unchanged---producing a static, narratively trivial sequence.
For the relay pattern ($A \to AB \to B$), a model that incorrectly preserves $A$ in all shots achieves IC $\approx 0.94$, while one that correctly removes $A$ from Shot~3 achieves IC $\approx 0.78$---a 17\% penalty for correct behavior.

We validate this across 3,600 videos generated by four models: IC and our PTM metric (\S\ref{sec:ptm_def}) produce opposite rankings at the model level ($r_{\text{IC-PTM}} = -0.998$).
VIC achieves highest IC (0.94) but lowest PTM (0.070); StoryDiffusion has highest PTM (0.243) but lower IC (0.81).
This implies that IC maximization inadvertently selects for narratively \emph{worse} models---a model that simply copies Shot~1 three times would achieve near-perfect IC while completely failing at narrative execution.
The paradox motivates the matrix-matching evaluation we introduce next.

\section{The DIAGONAL Framework}
\label{sec:framework}
%% ============================================================

We present \diagonal{} as a unified framework that formalizes multi-shot narrative dynamics as matrix algebra, defines a single evaluation object---the error matrix---that captures \emph{all} narrative failures, and instantiates it through a VLM-based detection pipeline with noise-robust metrics (Fig.~\ref{fig:metrics}).

\subsection{Narrative as State Transition Matrices}
\label{sec:formalization}

We denote a multi-shot video as a sequence of $T$ shots $V_1, V_2, \ldots, V_T$, each depicting a visual scene.
Each video involves $N$ named entities $e_1, e_2, \ldots, e_N$ (e.g., ``a chef,'' ``a dog'').
The \textbf{presence matrix} $S \in \{0,1\}^{T \times N}$ records whether each entity appears in each shot:
\[
S_{t,e} = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if entity } e \text{ is present in shot } t, \\ 0 & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases}
\]
For example, the relay narrative $A \to AB \to B$ has:
\[
S = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \text{(rows = shots, columns = entities).}
\]

The \textbf{state transition matrix} captures entity dynamics between consecutive shots:
\begin{equation}
\Delta S = S_{2:T} - S_{1:T\!-\!1},
\label{eq:delta_s}
\end{equation}
where each entry $\Delta S_{t,e} \in \{-1, 0, +1\}$ encodes an atomic operation at transition $t$: entity $e$ \emph{enters} ($+1$), \emph{exits} ($-1$), or \emph{holds} ($0$).
For the relay example: $\Delta S = \bigl[\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & +1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$, meaning $B$ enters at transition 1 and $A$ exits at transition 2.

We focus on $T = 3$ shots with $N \leq 3$ entities, yielding $\Delta S \in \{-1,0,+1\}^{2 \times 3}$.
This scope is chosen deliberately: $T = 3$ is the \emph{minimal complete narrative cycle}.
A 2-shot sequence allows only a single transition boundary, so entities can be introduced \emph{or} removed but not both---precluding narrative arcs.
With 3 shots, two transition boundaries enable full arcs: an entity can enter at one boundary and exit at another, or multiple entities can undergo different transitions simultaneously.
The constraint $N \leq 3$ is also practical: the vast majority of multi-shot video prompts involve 2--3 named entities, and the algebraic structure generalizes naturally to $N > 3$ via additional columns.

\subsection{Topological Basis and Completeness}
\label{sec:completeness}

We identify three \textbf{basis patterns} that represent fundamental narrative archetypes:

\textbf{Relay} ($\calB_1$): $A \to AB \to B$.
One entity hands off to another---$B$ enters while $A$ exits:
$\calB_1 = \bigl[\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & +1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$.

\textbf{Split} ($\calB_2$): $AB \to A \to B$.
Co-occurring entities separate into alternating scenes:
$\calB_2 = \bigl[\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ -1 & +1 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$.

\textbf{Accumulation} ($\calB_3$): $A \to AB \to ABC$.
Entities are progressively introduced:
$\calB_3 = \bigl[\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & +1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & +1 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$.

Four \textbf{derived patterns} are obtained by applying time-reversal (negating and reversing rows of $\Delta S$) and column permutation (swapping entity roles):
Reduction ($ABC \!\to\! AB \!\to\! A$),
Convergence ($A \!\to\! B \!\to\! AB$),
Reverse Relay ($B \!\to\! AB \!\to\! A$), and
Sliding Window ($AB \!\to\! BC \!\to\! C$)---the most demanding pattern with 4 non-zero transitions (Table~\ref{tab:pattern_dist}).

\begin{theorem}[Completeness of the Narrative Space]
Under column permutation and integer-coefficient linear combination, the two basis patterns $\calB_1$ (Relay) and $\calB_2$ (Split) generate all matrices in $\mathbb{M}_{2 \times 3}(\mathbb{Z})$.
\end{theorem}

Intuitively, this means that \emph{any conceivable 3-shot narrative}---no matter how complex the entity dynamics---can be decomposed into a combination of relays and splits (Fig.~\ref{fig:completeness}).

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
We show that all six ``elementary'' $2 \times 3$ matrices $E_{ij}$ (having 1 in position $(i,j)$ and 0 elsewhere) can be constructed from $\calB_1$ and $\calB_2$ using only column permutations and integer addition.
Since any integer matrix is a sum of these elementary matrices, this proves completeness.
The construction proceeds in three steps: (1)~column permutations of $\calB_1$ yield three variants whose linear combination gives $X = E_{11} - E_{21}$; (2)~adding $X$ to a permuted $\calB_2$ isolates $-E_{22}$; (3)~column permutations of $E_{22}$ and combinations with $X$ produce all remaining $E_{ij}$.
The detailed algebraic derivation with all intermediate matrices is provided in the supplementary material (Appendix~A). \qedhere
\end{proof}

\textbf{Implications.}
Theorem~1 has three important consequences.
\emph{First}, \diagonal{}'s seven patterns form a \emph{mathematically complete} basis: evaluating a model on these patterns is sufficient to characterize its behavior on \emph{any} narrative topology, including patterns not explicitly in the benchmark.
This distinguishes our framework from prior work~\cite{moviebench,locot2v,shotadapter} that selects patterns heuristically without coverage guarantees.
\emph{Second}, the theorem provides a \emph{minimality} argument: any benchmark claiming narrative completeness must include patterns that together span $\mathbb{M}_{2 \times 3}(\mathbb{Z})$; our two basis patterns achieve this optimally.
\emph{Third}, the result provides a principled framework for extension: $T$-shot narratives with $N$ entities require spanning $\mathbb{M}_{(T-1) \times N}(\mathbb{Z})$, achievable with $2(T-1)$ basis matrices, scaling linearly rather than combinatorially.
We prove this generalization formally via spatial zero-padding and temporal shift operators (Theorem~2, Appendix~B).

\subsection{Matrix Distance and Error Taxonomy}
\label{sec:error_taxonomy}

The formalism above defines the \emph{prescribed} transition matrix $\Delta S^*$ for any narrative pattern.
Given an observed presence matrix $S_\text{obs}$ (obtained by any detector; \S\ref{sec:vlm_instantiation}), the observed transition matrix follows identically:
\begin{equation}
\Delta S_\text{obs} = S_{\text{obs},\,2:T} - S_{\text{obs},\,1:T\!-\!1}.
\label{eq:delta_s_obs}
\end{equation}
The \textbf{error matrix} $E = \Delta S_\text{obs} - \Delta S^*$ is the single object that captures \emph{all} narrative failures:
\begin{equation}
E_{t,e} = \Delta S_{\text{obs},\,t,e} - \Delta S^*_{t,e}, \quad E \in \{-2,\ldots,+2\}^{(T-1) \times N}.
\label{eq:error_matrix}
\end{equation}
A perfect generation yields $E = \mathbf{0}$; every non-zero entry localizes a specific failure to a transition--entity pair $(t, e)$.
The \textbf{matrix distance} is the natural scalar summary:
\begin{equation}
d(S_\text{obs}, S^*) = \| E \|_F = \| \Delta S_\text{obs} - \Delta S^* \|_F.
\label{eq:matrix_distance}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Failure taxonomy from $E$.}
The algebraic structure of $E$ directly induces a complete failure classification without ad-hoc definitions.
Each non-zero entry $E_{t,e}$ falls into exactly one of four types determined by the prescribed value $\Delta S^*_{t,e}$ and the sign of $E_{t,e}$:
\begin{align}
\text{\textbf{Type~I} (Context Bleeding):} &\quad \Delta S^*_{t,e} = -1,\; E_{t,e} > 0 \label{eq:type1} \\
\text{\textbf{Type~II} (Amnesia):} &\quad \Delta S^*_{t,e} = +1,\; E_{t,e} < 0 \label{eq:type2} \\
\text{\textbf{Type~III} (Spurious):} &\quad \Delta S^*_{t,e} = 0,\;\; E_{t,e} \neq 0 \label{eq:type3} \\
\text{\textbf{Type~IV} (Reversal):} &\quad |E_{t,e}| = 2 \label{eq:type4}
\end{align}
Type~I captures an entity that should exit but persists ($E_{t,e} \in \{+1, +2\}$); Type~II captures an entity that should enter but fails to appear ($E_{t,e} \in \{-1, -2\}$); Type~III captures unprescribed transitions in hold cells; Type~IV is the most severe---the observed transition is in the \emph{opposite} direction from prescribed.
This taxonomy is exhaustive: every $E_{t,e} \neq 0$ maps to exactly one type, and the set of all non-zero entries in $E$ constitutes a complete diagnostic of the generation's narrative failures.

\subsection{VLM-Based Instantiation and Robust Evaluation}
\label{sec:vlm_instantiation}

\textbf{Entity detection.}
To construct $S_\text{obs}$ in practice, we extract the middle frame of each shot and query a VLM (Qwen2-VL-7B-Instruct~\cite{qwen2vl}) with a binary question per entity:
\begin{equation}
S_\text{obs}[t, k] = \mathbb{1}\!\bigl[\text{VLM}(\text{frame}_t, \text{``Is there a } e_k \text{ visible?''}) = \text{Yes}\bigr].
\label{eq:vlm_probe}
\end{equation}
Binary VLM queries avoid two limitations of continuous CLIP probes: (i)~CLIP entity probes yield present--absent gaps of only 0.008--0.019 (Cohen's $d = 0.23$--$0.59$), requiring fragile threshold tuning; (ii)~full-prompt CLIP similarity suffers from bag-of-words effects rendering shot-prompt discrimination near-random.
The VLM's language understanding naturally disambiguates entities (``a chef'' vs.\ ``a waiter'') without relying on visual-textual alignment quality.

\textbf{From matrix distance to robust metrics.}
The ideal evaluation would use the matrix distance $d = \|E\|_F$ directly.
However, VLM detection introduces systematic noise (TPR $= 52\%$, TNR $= 67\%$) that affects the error matrix non-uniformly.
Critically, the noise concentrates in \emph{hold cells} ($\Delta S^* = 0$), which constitute the majority of entries:
a false positive or false negative in a hold cell creates a spurious $E_{t,e} \neq 0$ entry that inflates $\|E\|_F$ identically for all methods.
We verify empirically that the raw Frobenius distance is \emph{not} discriminative between methods ($p > 0.4$ for all pairwise comparisons; Appendix).

This motivates decomposing the error matrix into two complementary projections that isolate the signal from noise:

\textbf{Prescribed Transition Match (PTM).}
\label{sec:ptm_def}
PTM restricts evaluation to the \emph{prescribed subspace}---the $\Delta S^* \neq 0$ cells where narrative-defining transitions occur:
\begin{equation}
\text{PTM} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{A}|} \sum_{(t,e) \in \mathcal{A}} \mathbb{1}\!\left[E_{t,e} = 0\right], \quad \mathcal{A} = \{(t,e) : \Delta S^*_{t,e} \neq 0\}.
\label{eq:ptm}
\end{equation}
Equivalently, PTM $= 1 - \frac{1}{|\mathcal{A}|}\sum_{(t,e) \in \mathcal{A}} \mathbb{1}[E_{t,e} \neq 0]$: it measures the fraction of the error matrix's prescribed entries that are zero.
By discarding hold cells, PTM eliminates the dominant noise source while retaining the entries that distinguish narrative success from failure.

\textbf{Entity Presence Accuracy (EPA).}
\label{sec:epa_def}
EPA operates on the presence matrix $S$ rather than its derivative $\Delta S$, providing a complementary view of \emph{static cast compliance}:
\begin{equation}
\text{EPA} = \frac{1}{TN}\sum_{t=1}^{T}\sum_{k=1}^{N} \mathbb{1}\!\left[S_{\text{obs},\,tk} = S^*_{tk}\right].
\label{eq:epa}
\end{equation}
While PTM evaluates whether the \emph{dynamics} are correct (did the right transitions happen?), EPA evaluates whether the \emph{states} are correct (are the right entities present in each shot?).
Together, PTM and EPA are not ad-hoc heuristic metrics but principled projections of the error matrix $E$: PTM projects onto the prescribed subspace of $\Delta S$, while EPA projects onto the full space of $S$.
Under perfect detection, both reduce to scalar summaries of $\|E\|_F$; under noisy detection, they provide strictly more discriminative power than the raw matrix distance by isolating signal from detector noise.

Basis patterns receive 200 prompts each; derived patterns receive 100 each (Table~\ref{tab:pattern_dist}), reflecting the algebraic structure where derived patterns are compositions of basis patterns.

\paragraph{Prompt design.}
Prompts are generated via Gemini~1.5~\cite{gemini} with structured templates specifying: narrative pattern, entities drawn from 20 categories (humans, animals, objects), scene themes from 10 environments, and per-shot descriptions.
Following~\cite{dalle3}, each scenario has three variants: \texttt{gen\_prompt} (quality tags for generation), \texttt{eval\_prompt} (clean descriptions for CLIP evaluation), and \texttt{diag\_prompt} (entity probes for presence detection).
Entity pairs have mean CLIP text similarity 0.23 ($\sigma = 0.08$); pairs with similarity $> 0.45$ are excluded to prevent ambiguous detections.

\paragraph{Quality control.}
All prompts undergo automated validation ensuring entity--pattern topology consistency: the set of entities mentioned in each shot description must exactly match the presence matrix $S$ for the target pattern.
Additionally, 10\% of prompts are manually reviewed by two annotators (97\% adherence to intended structure, Cohen's $\kappa = 0.91$).
Prompts that describe ambiguous scenarios (e.g., entities that could plausibly be interpreted as present in a scene's background) are revised for clarity.

\paragraph{Diversity.}
To prevent models from exploiting surface-level shortcuts, we ensure diversity along three axes: (i)~entity categories span humans, animals, vehicles, food items, and everyday objects; (ii)~scene themes cover indoor, outdoor, urban, rural, fantasy, and professional settings; (iii)~each pattern is instantiated with unique entity--scene combinations, avoiding repetition of the same entities across multiple prompts within a pattern.

\subsection{Human Evaluation}
\label{sec:human_eval}

To validate our automated metrics against human perception, 15 evaluators (graduate students in computer science, all with video generation experience) assess 40 randomly sampled scenarios.
Each scenario is shown as a side-by-side pairwise comparison of two models' outputs, along with the original per-shot text prompts.
Evaluators rate each pair on 5-point Likert scales for \emph{Narrative Compliance} (``Which video better follows the prescribed entity dynamics?'').
Each comparison is rated by 3 evaluators (120 total comparisons).

PTM rankings match human narrative judgments ($\rho = 0.80$, $p < 0.05$; Fleiss' $\kappa = 0.67$), validating it as a reliable automated proxy.
VIC ranks last in human narrative preference (17.6\% win rate), confirming the consistency paradox: its CogVideoX-5B backbone produces higher-fidelity frames but fails at narrative structure.
The high narrative tie rate (42.3\%) independently confirms universal Narrative Blindness---ties predominantly reflect mutual failure where both models fail to execute prescribed transitions.

\subsection{Discussion}

\paragraph{Why is Narrative Blindness universal?}
All four architectures fail at $>$98\% of videos despite spanning different paradigms.
We attribute this to a common assumption: multi-shot coherence is achieved through \emph{global} feature sharing (shared attention, in-context tokens, keyframe anchoring), which is inherently at odds with narrative dynamics requiring \emph{controlled dissimilarity}.
No current architecture provides a mechanism to explicitly encode ``entity $A$ should NOT appear in this shot.''

\paragraph{VLM detection limitations.}
The VLM achieves 52\% TPR and 67\% TNR on entity detection---imperfect, but \emph{systematically} imperfect across all methods.
The key property for a benchmark metric is not absolute accuracy but \emph{discriminative validity}: whether it separates good methods from bad.
PTM achieves this with overwhelming statistical significance ($H = 275$, $p < 10^{-59}$), non-overlapping confidence intervals, and effect sizes up to $d = 0.72$.
The 3.5$\times$ gap between SD and VIC is far larger than CLIP-based metrics ($<$0.06 gap).
We also evaluated Grounding DINO~\cite{groundingdino} (TNR $= 4\%$: detects everything) and smaller VLMs (2B: TPR $= 40\%$), confirming 7B VLM as the optimal detection backbone (Appendix).

\paragraph{Metric design.}
PTM focuses on prescribed transitions ($\Delta S \neq 0$) rather than the full matrix, because hold cells ($\Delta S = 0$) are dominated by detection noise common to all methods.
We verified that the normalized Frobenius distance $\|\Delta S_\text{obs} - \Delta S^*\|_F$ is \emph{not} discriminative ($p > 0.4$ for all pairs; Appendix), confirming that the prescribed-cell focus is essential for statistical power.

%% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ============================================================

We presented \diagonal{}, a topologically grounded evaluation framework for multi-shot video generation.
Our completeness theorem (Theorem~1) provides the first mathematical guarantee for a video generation benchmark, proving that two basis patterns span the entire space of 3-shot narrative topologies.
The VLM-based matrix matching pipeline provides clean binary presence matrices that enable direct computation of transition accuracy, yielding the Prescribed Transition Match (PTM)---a metric with overwhelming discriminative power ($H = 275$, $p < 10^{-59}$) and non-overlapping confidence intervals across all methods.
Evaluation of four architectures across 3,600 videos reveals a 3.5$\times$ PTM gap between the best (StoryDiffusion: 0.243) and worst (VIC: 0.070) models, with the consistency paradox ($r_{\text{IC-PTM}} = -0.998$) demonstrating that holistic metrics are adversarial to narrative evaluation.

\paragraph{Limitations and future work.}
\diagonal{} evaluates 3-shot sequences with $\leq$3 entities; Theorem~2 (Appendix~B) proves the formal generalization to $T$ shots and $N$ entities via linear operators.
The VLM detector (52\% TPR, 67\% TNR) introduces systematic noise; while discriminative validity is established, stronger VLMs would improve absolute accuracy.
VGoT (600/1K) did not complete all scenarios due to generation failures, which may introduce selection bias; however, metrics are mean-normalized per video.
We release all prompts, evaluation code, and 3,600 generated videos for reproducibility.
