\begin{abstract}
Text-to-multi-shot video (T2MSV) generation models are evaluated primarily on visual quality and frame-level consistency, yet these metrics fail to capture whether models can execute \emph{narrative dynamics}---the structured entry and exit of entities across shots.
We formalize multi-shot narratives as \emph{state transition matrices} $\Delta S \in \{-1,0,+1\}^{(T-1) \times N}$, where each element encodes an atomic operation: entry ($+1$), exit ($-1$), or hold ($0$).
We prove that all possible 3-shot narrative topologies can be spanned by just two basis matrices under column permutation and linear combination (\textbf{Theorem~1}), establishing mathematical completeness.
Building on this, we introduce \diagonal{}, a benchmark comprising 1,000 prompts spanning 7 narrative patterns (3 basis, 4 derived) with complementary metrics: Narrative Alignment Score, Diagonal Dominance, State Transition Accuracy, Entity Presence Accuracy, and SVD Entropy.
Evaluation of four SOTA T2MSV architectures reveals that \emph{no model achieves positive diagonal dominance on any pattern} ($\epsilon$-pass $= 0\%$), exposing a fundamental limitation we term \textbf{Narrative Blindness}.
We identify four systematic failure types---Context Bleeding (VIC: 60.8\%), Amnesia (VGoT: 66.7\%), Attention Reversion, and Identity Bleeding---and validate via human evaluation ($\rho = 0.80$ with STA).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ============================================================

Diffusion-based generative models have rapidly expanded the capabilities of video synthesis, enabling a transition from single-clip generation to text-to-multi-shot video (T2MSV) generation~\cite{ho2022video,svd,imagenvideo}.
Recent systems such as StoryDiffusion~\cite{storydiff}, EchoShot~\cite{echoshot}, Video-In-Context (VIC)~\cite{vic}, and VGoT~\cite{vgot} can generate temporally connected sequences of scenes from textual descriptions, suggesting that multi-shot video generation is approaching practical usability.
These systems leverage diverse architectural strategies---consistent self-attention, flow-matching conditioning, in-context learning, and cascaded T2I-to-I2V pipelines---to maintain coherence across generated shots, and have demonstrated impressive visual fidelity in recent evaluations~\cite{vbench,vbench2}.

However, the apparent success of these systems is largely driven by evaluation protocols that overlook a fundamental aspect of narrative structure.
Existing benchmarks~\cite{vbench,vbench2,t2vcompbench,moviebench} evaluate identity consistency, visual quality, or single-clip compositionality, but none systematically test whether models can execute the \emph{narrative dynamics} that define cinematic storytelling: the structured introduction, co-occurrence, and removal of entities across shots.
This gap is particularly concerning because real-world video narratives---from films~\cite{moviebench} to advertisements to educational content---are fundamentally defined by \emph{who appears, when, and alongside whom}.
Without metrics that capture these dynamics, we cannot meaningfully assess whether T2MSV models are progressing toward their intended purpose.

Consider a simple relay narrative: $A \to AB \to B$, where entity $A$ appears alone, both $A$ and $B$ co-occur, then only $B$ remains.
This requires three distinct capabilities: (1)~introducing $B$ while preserving $A$, (2)~maintaining co-occurrence fidelity, and (3)~removing $A$ while $B$ persists.
As we demonstrate, current models systematically fail at capability~(3)---the departed entity ``bleeds'' into subsequent shots (\textbf{Context Bleeding}).
Critically, existing holistic metrics like Identity Consistency (IC)~\cite{shotadapter} \emph{reward} this failure because persistent entities increase cross-shot similarity.
We term this the \textbf{consistency paradox}: IC is adversarial to narrative compliance for any non-trivial pattern.
This paradox is not merely academic---it implies that the community may be inadvertently optimizing models \emph{away from} narrative competence by chasing IC improvements.

To address these limitations, we formalize entity dynamics as a \emph{state transition matrix} $\Delta S \in \{-1,0,+1\}^{2 \times 3}$ and prove that \textbf{all} 3-shot topologies are generated from two basis matrices via permutation and linear combination (\textbf{Theorem~1}).
Building on this formalization, \diagonal{} contributes:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
  \item A \textbf{topological formalization} of multi-shot narratives via state transition matrices with a mathematical completeness guarantee: we prove that two basis patterns span the entire space of 3-shot narrative topologies (\S\ref{sec:formalization}).
  \item A \textbf{1,000-prompt benchmark} (DM-1K) spanning 7 narrative patterns (3~basis, 4~derived) with five decomposed metrics---NAS, DD, STA, EPA, SVD Entropy---that isolate different aspects of narrative compliance (\S\ref{sec:metrics}).
  \item A \textbf{four-type failure taxonomy} (Context Bleeding, Amnesia, Attention Reversion, Identity Bleeding) that provides actionable architectural diagnostics (\S\ref{sec:failures}).
  \item The first formal identification of the \textbf{consistency paradox}: we prove analytically and validate empirically ($r = -0.34$) that holistic similarity metrics are adversarial to narrative evaluation (\S\ref{sec:blindspot}).
  \item Comprehensive evaluation of four SOTA T2MSV architectures spanning all major design paradigms, revealing universal \textbf{Narrative Blindness} ($\epsilon$-pass $= 0\%$) and validated by human evaluation ($\rho = 0.80$) (\S\ref{sec:experiments}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%% ============================================================

\paragraph{Text-to-video generation.}
The foundation of modern video synthesis rests on diffusion models~\cite{ddpm,ldm,scoreflow}, which have been extended to the temporal domain through various architectures.
Early approaches employed UNet backbones with temporal attention layers~\cite{svd,imagenvideo,ho2022video}, while recent work has shifted toward scalable DiT architectures~\cite{dit,wan,sora} with transformer-based backbones~\cite{attention,vit}, flow-matching objectives~\cite{flowmatching}, and classifier-free guidance~\cite{cfg}.
Commercial and open-source systems including Sora~\cite{sora}, Movie Gen~\cite{moviegen}, and Open-Sora~\cite{opensora} have demonstrated unprecedented single-clip quality, but multi-shot generation---producing sequences of narratively connected clips---remains a distinct and largely unsolved challenge.

\paragraph{Multi-shot video generation.}
Multi-shot extensions address narrative-level coherence via diverse mechanisms: shared self-attention~\cite{storydiff}, cross-attention conditioning~\cite{videostudio}, latent initialization~\cite{mevg}, LLM-guided layouts~\cite{vgot,videodirectorgpt}, keyframe transitions~\cite{seine,dynamicrafter}, flow-matching portraits~\cite{echoshot}, IP-Adapter conditioning~\cite{ip_adapter}, and in-context LoRA~\cite{vic}.
AnimateDiff~\cite{animatediff} and Stable Video Diffusion~\cite{svd} provide general-purpose motion modules but do not natively support multi-shot narrative control.
SeedStory~\cite{minidalle} extends multimodal LLMs for long-form visual storytelling but evaluates on image sequences rather than continuous video.
These methods span four architectural paradigms: (1)~Consistent Self-Attention, where StoryDiffusion~\cite{storydiff} shares attention maps to enforce visual coherence; (2)~flow-matching, where EchoShot~\cite{echoshot} uses spatio-temporal echo guidance on Wan2.1~\cite{wan}; (3)~in-context DiT, where VIC~\cite{vic} fine-tunes CogVideoX~\cite{cogvideox} with LoRA for multi-shot in-context generation; and (4)~cascaded T2I+I2V, where VGoT~\cite{vgot} uses Kolors~\cite{kolors} for keyframe synthesis and DynamiCrafter~\cite{dynamicrafter} for video interpolation.
We evaluate representatives from each paradigm, providing the first systematic cross-paradigm comparison on narrative dynamics.

\paragraph{Video generation benchmarks.}
Existing benchmarks quantify consistency via embedding similarity (Table~\ref{tab:benchmark}).
VBench~\cite{vbench} and VBench 2.0~\cite{vbench2} compute frame-level CLIP~\cite{clip} and DINO~\cite{dinov2} similarities that conflate subject and background; T2V-CompBench~\cite{t2vcompbench} targets single-clip compositionality without multi-shot transitions.
ShotAdapter~\cite{shotadapter} isolates subject regions via IC, but was designed for single-entity shots---applying IC to multi-entity scenes produces a \emph{chimera embedding} that conflates co-occurring entities, rendering entity-removal failures invisible.
MovieBench~\cite{moviebench} proposes hierarchical evaluation for long videos but relies on VLM-based scoring without entity-level decomposition.
LoCoT2V-Bench~\cite{locot2v} focuses on long-form complex prompts but lacks systematic multi-shot transition evaluation and mathematical formalization.
Video-Bench~\cite{videobench} targets video understanding rather than generation evaluation.
Traditional metrics like FID~\cite{fid} and FVD~\cite{fvd} measure distributional fidelity but are agnostic to narrative structure.
To our knowledge, \diagonal{} is the first benchmark to formalize multi-shot narratives via state transition matrices and provide topological completeness guarantees.

\section{The Consistency Paradox}
\label{sec:blindspot}
%% ============================================================

Before introducing our framework, we demonstrate a structural flaw in existing evaluation: holistic similarity metrics systematically \emph{reward} narrative failures.

\paragraph{Formal analysis.}
ShotAdapter's Identity Consistency (IC)~\cite{shotadapter} computes mean pairwise CLIP similarity across all shots: $\text{IC} = \frac{1}{\binom{T}{2}} \sum_{i<j} \cos(\Phi_V(V_i), \Phi_V(V_j))$.
When a narrative requires entity changes (e.g., $A \to AB \to B$), a model that correctly removes entity $A$ will produce \emph{lower} cross-shot similarity than one that incorrectly preserves $A$ in all shots.
IC thus penalizes correct narrative execution.

Formally, for any pattern with $\|\Delta S\|_F > 0$ (i.e., at least one entity transition), the IC-maximizing generation has $\Delta S_{\text{gen}} = \mathbf{0}$---the static sequence ignoring all dynamics.
This is because $\cos(\Phi_V(V_i), \Phi_V(V_j))$ is maximized when $V_i$ and $V_j$ are identical, which requires no entity changes between shots.
For the relay pattern specifically, a model that preserves $A$ in all three shots achieves IC $\approx 0.94$, while a model that correctly removes $A$ from Shot~3 achieves IC $\approx 0.78$---a 17\% penalty for correct behavior.

\paragraph{Empirical validation.}
We validate this analysis across 3,049 generated videos spanning all four architectures and seven patterns.
IC and STA exhibit a Pearson correlation of $r = -0.34$ ($p < 10^{-5}$), confirming the predicted adversarial relationship.
The correlation is strongest for exit-heavy patterns (Relay: $r = -0.41$, Reduction: $r = -0.38$) and weaker for entry-only patterns (Accumulation: $r = -0.19$), consistent with our analysis since exits cause larger embedding shifts than entries.
VIC achieves the highest IC (0.94) but the \emph{lowest} STA (0.431), precisely because its in-context conditioning preserves all entities regardless of instructions.
StoryDiffusion---highest STA (0.630)---records lower IC (0.81) because successful entity removals reduce cross-shot similarity.

\paragraph{Implications.}
This finding has profound implications for the field: prior work reporting ``improved identity consistency'' may be inadvertently selecting for models that are \emph{worse} at narrative dynamics.
Model development guided by IC maximization will converge toward architectures that produce static, unchanging sequences---the antithesis of narrative video generation.
This motivates the decomposed metrics we introduce in \S\ref{sec:metrics}, which separately evaluate entity presence, state transitions, and narrative alignment without conflating visual similarity with narrative fidelity.

\section{Topological Formalization}
\label{sec:formalization}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{State Transition Matrix}

We represent a multi-shot video as a sequence of $T$ shots $(V_1, \ldots, V_T)$ with $N$ named entities $\{e_1, \ldots, e_N\}$.
The \emph{presence matrix} $S \in \{0,1\}^{T \times N}$ encodes which entities appear in which shots: $S_{t,e} = 1$ iff entity $e$ is present in shot $t$.
The temporal evolution of entities is then captured by the \emph{state transition matrix}:
\begin{equation}
\Delta S = S_{2:T} - S_{1:T-1} \in \mathbb{M}_{(T-1) \times N}\!\bigl(\{-1, 0, +1\}\bigr),
\label{eq:delta_s}
\end{equation}
where $\Delta S_{t,e}$ records the state change of entity $e$ from shot $t$ to $t\!+\!1$: entry ($+1$), exit ($-1$), or hold ($0$).
We focus on $T\!=\!3, N\!\leq\!3$, yielding a $2 \times 3$ matrix capturing the minimal complete narrative cycle.
This choice is motivated by the observation that 3-shot sequences are the minimal unit requiring both entity introduction and removal---2-shot sequences can only introduce or remove, never both.

\subsection{Basis and Derived Patterns}

Three \emph{basis patterns} represent fundamental archetypes:

\textbf{Relay} ($\calB_1$: $A \!\to\! AB \!\to\! B$). Narrative handoff---$B$ enters while $A$ exits:
$\calB_1 = \bigl[\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & +1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr] = E_{12} - E_{21}$.

\textbf{Split} ($\calB_2$: $AB \!\to\! A \!\to\! B$). Co-occurring entities separate:
$\calB_2 = \bigl[\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ -1 & +1 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr] = -E_{12} - E_{21} + E_{22}$.

\textbf{Accumulation} ($\calB_3$: $A \!\to\! AB \!\to\! ABC$). Progressive introduction:
$\calB_3 = \bigl[\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & +1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & +1 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr] = E_{12} + E_{23}$.

Four \emph{derived patterns} are obtained via time-reversal $\calT$ (negate and reverse rows) and column permutation $\calP_{ij}$:

\textbf{Reduction} ($ABC \!\to\! AB \!\to\! A$) $= \calT(\calB_3)$.
Progressive entity removal---the temporal mirror of Accumulation.
$\Delta S = \bigl[\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & 0 & -1 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$.

\textbf{Convergence} ($A \!\to\! B \!\to\! AB$) $= \calP_{AB}(\calT(\calB_2))$.
Separate entities converge into co-occurrence.
$\Delta S = \bigl[\begin{smallmatrix} -1 & +1 \\ +1 & 0 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$.

\textbf{Reverse Relay} ($B \!\to\! AB \!\to\! A$) $= \calP_{AB}(\calB_1)$.
Entity permutation of Relay with reversed roles.

\textbf{Sliding Window} ($AB \!\to\! BC \!\to\! C$) $=$ superposition of relay and exit---the most demanding pattern with 4 transitions.
$\Delta S = \bigl[\begin{smallmatrix} -1 & 0 & +1 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr]$.

\subsection{Completeness Theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Completeness of the Narrative Space]
Under column permutation $\calP_{ij}$ and integer-coefficient linear combination, $\calB_1$ (Relay) and $\calB_2$ (Split) span $\mathbb{M}_{2 \times 3}(\mathbb{Z})$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We construct all six standard basis matrices $E_{ij}$ from $\calB_1$ and $\calB_2$.

\textbf{Step~1.}
Define $v_1 = \calB_1 = E_{12} - E_{21}$,
$v_2 = \calP_{13}(\calB_1) = E_{12} - E_{23}$,
$v_3 = \calP_{23}(\calP_{12}(\calB_1)) = E_{11} - E_{23}$.
Then $X = v_3 - v_2 + v_1 = E_{11} - E_{21}$.

\textbf{Step~2.}
$X + \calP_{12}(\calB_2) = (E_{11} - E_{21}) + (-E_{11} + E_{21} - E_{22}) = -E_{22}$.

\textbf{Step~3.}
From $E_{22}$, permutations yield $E_{21}, E_{23}$.
From $X + E_{21}$: $E_{11}$.
Further permutations: $E_{12}, E_{13}$.
All six $\{E_{ij}\}$ obtained; any $\Delta S \in \mathbb{M}_{2 \times 3}(\mathbb{Z})$ is their integer combination.
\end{proof}

\textbf{Implication.}
Theorem~1 guarantees that \diagonal{} is \emph{mathematically complete}: any conceivable 3-shot narrative topology is expressible as a linear combination of our basis patterns.
This distinguishes our benchmark from prior work~\cite{moviebench,locot2v,shotadapter} that selects narrative types heuristically without formal coverage guarantees.
Moreover, the completeness result provides a principled framework for extending to longer sequences: $T$-shot narratives with $N$ entities require spanning $\mathbb{M}_{(T-1) \times N}(\mathbb{Z})$, and an analogous construction yields a basis of $2(T-1)$ matrices.

\section{The DIAGONAL Evaluation Framework}
\label{sec:metrics}
%% ============================================================

We define complementary metrics that decompose multi-shot narrative alignment into interpretable components.

\subsection{Cross-Modal Similarity Matrix}

For a generated video with 3 shots and evaluation prompts $\{T_1, T_2, T_3\}$:
\begin{equation}
\Mobs[i,j] = \cos\!\bigl(\Phi_V(V_i),\; \Phi_T(T_j)\bigr),
\label{eq:mobs}
\end{equation}
where $\Phi_V$ and $\Phi_T$ are CLIP ViT-L/14 visual and text encoders.
Each shot $V_i$ is represented by the mean-pooled feature of 5 uniformly sampled frames.

\subsection{Narrative Alignment Score (NAS)}
Row-normalized alignment measuring per-shot prompt specificity:
\begin{equation}
\text{NAS} = \frac{1}{3} \sum_{i=1}^{3} \frac{\Mobs[i,i]}{\sum_{j=1}^{3} \Mobs[i,j]}.
\label{eq:nas}
\end{equation}
NAS $\in [0, 1]$, with $\text{NAS} = 1/3$ for a model producing shots equally similar to all prompts (random baseline) and $\text{NAS} = 1$ for perfect prompt specificity.
Row normalization ensures NAS is invariant to absolute CLIP similarity magnitudes, isolating the \emph{relative} alignment quality.

\subsection{Diagonal Dominance (DD)}
Whether the correct prompt achieves highest similarity:
\begin{equation}
\text{DD}_i = \Mobs[i,i] - \max_{j \neq i} \Mobs[i,j].
\label{eq:dd}
\end{equation}
We define the $\epsilon$-\emph{pass rate} as the fraction of videos where DD$_i > \epsilon$ for all $i$, using $\epsilon = 0.02$ to account for CLIP measurement noise.
DD $> 0$ means each shot aligns best with its intended prompt; DD $< 0$ indicates systematic misalignment where shots are more similar to the wrong prompts.
Unlike NAS which measures relative alignment magnitude, DD captures a binary property: whether the model can produce \emph{distinctive} shots that are unambiguously matched to their prompts.

\subsection{Entity Presence Accuracy (EPA)}
We design entity-specific CLIP probes to detect individual entity presence:
\begin{equation}
E[i,k] = \cos\!\bigl(\Phi_V(V_i),\; \Phi_T(\text{``a photo of } e_k\text{''})\bigr).
\end{equation}
Entity presence is binarized via per-shot median threshold: $\hat{P}_{ik} = \mathbb{1}[E[i,k] > \text{median}_k(E[i,\cdot])]$.
EPA then measures the agreement between detected and prescribed presence:
\begin{equation}
\text{EPA} = \frac{1}{3N}\sum_{i,k} \mathbb{1}\!\left[\hat{P}_{ik} = P^*_{ik}\right],
\label{eq:epa}
\end{equation}
where $P^*_{ik}$ is the ground-truth presence from the pattern specification.
EPA captures both false positives (entity detected when it should be absent) and false negatives (entity not detected when it should be present), making it sensitive to both Context Bleeding and Amnesia failures.

\subsection{State Transition Accuracy (STA)}
While EPA evaluates static presence per shot, STA measures the accuracy of \emph{dynamic} transitions between shots:
\begin{equation}
\text{STA} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{A}|} \sum_{\substack{(t,e) \in \mathcal{A}}} \mathbb{1}\!\left[\text{sgn}(\Delta E_{t,e}) = \text{sgn}(\Delta S_{t,e})\right],
\label{eq:sta}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{A} = \{(t,e) : \Delta S_{t,e} \neq 0\}$ is the set of prescribed transitions and $\Delta E_{t,e} = E[t\!+\!1,e] - E[t,e]$ is the observed entity score change.
STA isolates the most challenging aspect of narrative generation: correctly executing state transitions.
A model that generates identical shots receives STA $= 0$ for any pattern with $|\mathcal{A}| > 0$, making STA the strictest narrative fidelity metric in our suite.
Note that STA evaluates only the transition \emph{direction} (entry/exit), not magnitude, making it robust to CLIP sensitivity variations across entity types.

\subsection{SVD Entropy}
Per-shot semantic complexity is measured via SVD on the frame-level CLIP feature matrix $F_t \in \mathbb{R}^{5 \times d}$ (5~frames $\times$ CLIP dimension):
\begin{equation}
\calH(V_t) = -\sum_i \tilde{\sigma}_i \log \tilde{\sigma}_i, \quad \tilde{\sigma}_i = \sigma_i / {\textstyle\sum_j} \sigma_j,
\label{eq:svd_entropy}
\end{equation}
where $\{\sigma_i\}$ are the singular values of $F_t$.
Higher entropy indicates more diverse semantic content within a shot (multiple distinct entities), while lower entropy indicates semantic uniformity (single entity or homogeneous content).
The \emph{trajectory correctness rate} measures whether $\Delta\calH$ between consecutive shots matches the expected direction from entity count changes: $\calH$ should increase when entities are added and decrease when entities are removed.
This metric probes whether models adjust their generative diversity in response to narrative instructions, independent of whether the correct entities are generated.

\subsection{Failure Taxonomy}
Four types based on discrepancies between $\Delta S$ and observed changes:

\textbf{Type~I: Context Bleeding} ($\Delta S\!=\!-1$, entity persists).
The departed entity's visual signature remains in subsequent shots.

\textbf{Type~II: Amnesia} ($\Delta S\!=\!+1$, entity absent).
A new entity fails to appear despite explicit prompt instruction.

\textbf{Type~III: Attention Reversion.}
The diagonal shows a U-shape ($M_{11} > M_{22} < M_{33}$), indicating reversion to initial content.

\textbf{Type~IV: Identity Bleeding.}
Near-uniform similarity rows indicate failure to distinguish entities visually.

\subsection{Design Properties}

Our metric suite satisfies three desirable properties for narrative evaluation:

\textbf{Decomposability.}
Each metric isolates a distinct failure mode: NAS captures global alignment, DD measures prompt specificity, STA evaluates transition dynamics, EPA assesses entity presence, and SVD Entropy tracks semantic complexity.
Together, they provide a complete diagnostic profile; individually, they pinpoint specific failure types.

\textbf{Monotonicity.}
Each metric is monotonically related to the quality it measures: increasing STA implies better transition execution, increasing EPA implies more accurate entity rendering.
This enables unambiguous model comparison, unlike IC which is non-monotonic for narrative-relevant patterns (\S\ref{sec:blindspot}).

\textbf{Interpretability.}
All metrics have clear semantic meaning and bounded range ($[0, 1]$ for NAS, STA, EPA; $\mathbb{R}$ for DD and $\calH$), enabling intuitive comparison across models and patterns.
The failure taxonomy provides actionable diagnostic information: knowing that a model suffers primarily from Context Bleeding (rather than Amnesia) directly suggests architectural modifications.

This distribution ensures sufficient statistical power for per-pattern analysis while maintaining balanced representation of the three basis archetypes that span the complete narrative space.

\paragraph{Prompt design.}
Prompts are programmatically generated via Google Gemini~1.5~\cite{gemini} with structured templates specifying:
(1)~narrative pattern and entity assignments,
(2)~core entities drawn from 20 semantic categories spanning humans (occupations, ages, genders), animals (domestic, wild, mythical), and objects (vehicles, instruments),
(3)~scene themes from 10 distinct environments (urban, rural, interior, coastal, mountain, forest, desert, studio, fantasy, underwater), and
(4)~per-shot textual descriptions embedding entity dynamics naturally within coherent scene narratives.

Following best practices from caption-based generation~\cite{dalle3}, each scenario contains three prompt variants serving distinct purposes: \texttt{gen\_prompt} includes quality-enhancing tags (e.g., ``4K, cinematic lighting, detailed'') for generation; \texttt{eval\_prompt} provides clean scene descriptions for unbiased CLIP evaluation; and \texttt{diag\_prompt} contains entity-focused probes (e.g., ``a firefighter standing'') for fine-grained presence detection.
Entity pairs are selected for visual distinguishability, with mean CLIP text similarity of 0.23 ($\sigma = 0.08$), ensuring that entity detection is not confounded by semantic overlap.
We exclude entity pairs with CLIP text similarity $> 0.45$ to prevent ambiguous detections.
Additional details including sample prompts and generation statistics are provided in the supplementary material.

\paragraph{Quality control.}
All 1,000 prompts undergo automated validation: (1)~entity names match the prescribed pattern topology, (2)~each shot description contains exactly the specified entities, and (3)~no entity appears in shots where it should be absent.
We additionally perform manual spot-checking on a random 10\% subset to verify narrative naturalness and semantic coherence, finding 97\% adherence to the intended pattern structure.

\paragraph{Models.}
We evaluate four T2MSV architectures spanning all major design paradigms (Table~\ref{tab:models}):

\textbf{EchoShot}~\cite{echoshot} uses spatio-temporal echo guidance on Wan2.1-1.3B~\cite{wan} with flow-matching~\cite{flowmatching} (50 steps).
\textbf{VIC}~\cite{vic} fine-tunes CogVideoX-5B~\cite{cogvideox} with LoRA~\cite{lora} for in-context multi-shot generation (50 steps).
\textbf{VGoT}~\cite{vgot} cascades Kolors~\cite{kolors} T2I with DynamiCrafter~\cite{dynamicrafter} I2V through LLM-guided planning (25+50 steps).
All models use seed 42 for reproducibility.

\paragraph{Evaluation pipeline.}
Five uniformly-spaced frames per shot are extracted and processed by CLIP ViT-L/14~\cite{clip} (336$\times$336 resolution).
Per-shot features are mean-pooled across the 5 frames to produce a single embedding per shot.
The cross-modal similarity matrix $\Mobs$ is computed between shot embeddings and three prompt variants (eval and diagnostic).
Entity presence is binarized via a per-shot median threshold on CLIP entity probe scores.
All experiments are conducted on 4$\times$NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPUs; total evaluation time for 3,049 videos is approximately 4 hours.
Detailed threshold sensitivity analysis is provided in the supplementary material.

Table~\ref{tab:main} reveals several key findings.

\textbf{Finding 1: Universal Narrative Blindness.}
All four models exhibit DD $< 0$ across all patterns, with $\epsilon$-pass rate of 0.0\%.
No model consistently produces shots that are most similar to their intended prompts---a striking failure given that this is the minimal requirement for narrative coherence.
The mean DD across models ($-0.011$) indicates that off-diagonal similarities consistently exceed diagonal ones, meaning generated shots are systematically \emph{more aligned} with the wrong prompts than the intended ones.

\textbf{Finding 2: The consistency paradox confirmed.}
VIC achieves the highest NAS (0.703) but the \emph{lowest} STA (0.431), confirming that in-context conditioning produces visually coherent but narratively incorrect sequences.
This gap ($\Delta = 0.272$) is the largest among all models, underscoring the severity of the paradox for architectures that prioritize cross-shot feature sharing.

\textbf{Finding 3: StoryDiffusion leads in narrative accuracy.}
StoryDiffusion achieves the highest STA (0.630) and EPA (0.573), indicating that CSA, while imperfect, best preserves entity state transitions.
Its approach of sharing attention maps---rather than directly conditioning on prior frames---allows more selective entity management.

\textbf{Finding 4: EchoShot best tracks semantic complexity.}
EchoShot's SVD trajectory correctness (70.9\%) indicates its flow-matching architecture most faithfully reflects entity count changes in semantic diversity, likely because the continuous-time formulation~\cite{flowmatching} allows finer-grained control over generative trajectories.

Type~IV (Identity Bleeding) is highest in StoryDiffusion (21.7\%), where CSA's cross-shot feature sharing homogenizes entity appearances, making it difficult for CLIP to distinguish between entities that the model has rendered with near-identical visual features.

Fig.~\ref{fig:pareto} reveals a \emph{coherence--compliance trade-off} that we term the \textbf{Narrative Pareto Dilemma}.
In the NAS--STA plane, VIC occupies the high-coherence/low-compliance corner (NAS $= 0.703$, STA $= 0.431$), while StoryDiffusion offers the best balance (NAS $= 0.699$, STA $= 0.630$).
The empty upper-right quadrant---high NAS \emph{and} high STA---represents the ideal operating point that no current architecture achieves.

This trade-off is not accidental but reflects a fundamental tension in current conditioning mechanisms.
Architectures that enforce global cross-shot feature sharing (VIC's in-context conditioning) maximize visual coherence but destroy the localized entity control needed for narrative compliance.
Architectures with more selective feature sharing (StoryDiffusion's CSA) sacrifice some visual coherence but permit better entity management.
In the $\sdiag$--DD plane, all models cluster tightly at $\sdiag \approx 0.90$ with DD $\in [-0.012, -0.009]$, confirming that diagonal dominance is universally violated regardless of how well individual entity presence is detected.
Bridging this gap likely requires fundamentally new entity-aware conditioning mechanisms---such as entity slot architectures~\cite{videostudio} or narrative-conditioned classifier-free guidance---that can independently manage entity presence without compromising visual coherence.

\subsection{Cross-Metric Correlation Analysis}

To understand the relationships between our metrics, we compute pairwise Pearson correlations across all 3,049 videos.
NAS and $\sdiag$ are strongly correlated ($r = 0.82$), confirming that both capture aspects of shot-prompt alignment but from different perspectives (normalized vs.\ absolute).
STA and EPA show moderate positive correlation ($r = 0.58$), reflecting their complementary roles: EPA measures \emph{static} entity presence while STA evaluates \emph{dynamic} transitions.
The weak correlation between NAS and STA ($r = 0.31$) confirms that visual alignment (how well shots match prompts overall) is largely orthogonal to transition accuracy (whether entity changes are correct)---a model can produce shots that ``look right'' individually while failing to execute transitions between them.
DD shows the strongest correlation with human narrative judgments ($r = 0.72$) among all individual metrics, suggesting it captures the most salient aspect of perceived narrative quality: whether each shot is distinctively aligned with its intended prompt.
Bootstrap confidence intervals for all correlation estimates are provided in the supplementary material.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ============================================================

We have presented \diagonal{}, a topologically grounded evaluation framework for multi-shot video generation that formalizes narrative dynamics as state transition matrices.
Our key theoretical contribution---proving that two basis patterns span the complete narrative space (Theorem~1)---provides the first mathematical completeness guarantee for a video generation benchmark.
The framework introduces five complementary metrics (NAS, DD, STA, EPA, SVD Entropy) that decompose narrative alignment into interpretable components, and a four-type failure taxonomy that pinpoints architecture-specific vulnerabilities.

Evaluation of four SOTA architectures spanning all major T2MSV paradigms reveals universal \textbf{Narrative Blindness}: no model achieves positive diagonal dominance on any pattern ($\epsilon$-pass $= 0\%$), and 77--91\% of generated videos exhibit at least one systematic failure type.
The failure taxonomy reveals distinct architectural bottlenecks: VIC suffers from Context Bleeding (60.8\%) because its in-context conditioning cannot selectively suppress entity features; VGoT from Amnesia (66.7\%) due to planning-rendering decoupling where the LLM plans correctly but the I2V model fails to render new entities; and StoryDiffusion from Identity Bleeding (21.7\%) because CSA promotes visual homogeneity at the expense of entity distinguishability.
We further expose the \emph{consistency paradox}---holistic similarity metrics systematically reward narrative failures---validated analytically ($r = -0.34$ between IC and STA) and through human evaluation ($\rho = 0.80$ with STA).

Our results suggest that progress requires fundamentally new approaches to compositional entity control.
Future architectures must independently manage entity introduction, persistence, and removal within a unified generation framework, rather than relying on holistic conditioning that conflates narrative structure with visual consistency.
Promising directions include: (1)~entity-specific attention masks that selectively activate or suppress entity-associated features at each shot boundary, (2)~narrative-conditioned classifier-free guidance where the guidance scale varies per entity and per shot, and (3)~entity slot architectures that decouple identity representation from presence indicators, allowing the model to ``know'' an entity without generating it.

\paragraph{Limitations and future work.}
\diagonal{} currently evaluates 3-shot sequences with up to 3 entities; extension to variable-length narratives with branching entity timelines and hierarchical structures (e.g., scene-level and act-level dynamics) is a natural next step.
Our CLIP-based metrics capture coarse-grained entity presence at the semantic level; combining with detection-based methods (e.g., GroundingDINO~\cite{groundingdino}) or segmentation models could improve spatial granularity and enable fine-grained entity localization.
VIC (449/1000) and VGoT (600/1000) did not complete all scenarios; results should be interpreted with this caveat, though the lower completion rates are themselves informative of architectural limitations.
We release our full benchmark, evaluation code, and all 3,049 generated videos to facilitate reproducible research and enable the community to develop narrative-aware video generation models.
