\begin{abstract}
Multi-shot video generation models can now produce visually coherent sequences, yet they face a fundamental \emph{coherence--compliance trade-off}: the mechanisms that maintain visual consistency across shots---shared attention, in-context conditioning---prevent models from executing the dynamic entity entrances and exits that define storytelling.
Current evaluation paradigms, heavily optimized for visual quality and visual consistency, systematically overlook this trade-off by reducing complex narrative dynamics to simple frame-by-frame image comparisons.
We present the \diagonal{} framework, which formalizes multi-entity, multi-shot narratives into state transition matrices that algebraically encode character entrances and exits. 
Crucially, we prove that \textbf{every conceivable narrative topology} can be generated from just two fundamental basis patterns (Relay and Split) by applying intuitive narrative operations---such as time reversal and character swapping---along with pattern combinations (\textbf{Theorem~\ref{thm:completeness}}).
This elevates video evaluation from heuristic prompting to \emph{mathematically complete formal verification}.
To evaluate generated videos, we introduce \stem{} (State Transition Error Matching). Instead of relying on scalar similarities, \stem{} constructs an error matrix that isolates all narrative deviations by comparing the observed transitions against the prescribed script. This matrix is then density-normalized to decouple detector noise from true narrative signals, yielding two complementary metrics: \emph{Prescribed Transition Match} (PTM) for microscopic transition fidelity and \emph{Entity Presence Accuracy} (EPA) for macroscopic cast compliance.
On the MEMS-1K benchmark across four major video generation architectures, PTM clearly discriminates model performances, revealing a vast capability gap and a consistent rank reversal against Identity Consistency---empirically exposing the previously invisible architectural tension.
Furthermore, the algebraic structure of the error matrix naturally induces a comprehensive taxonomy of narrative failures (e.g., characters failing to exit or missing their entrances), providing structured, actionable diagnostics beyond simple scalar scores.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ============================================================

Multi-shot video generation models now produce visually compelling sequences~\cite{sora,storydiff,moviegen,ho2022video,svd,imagenvideo}, yet they face a fundamental \emph{coherence--compliance trade-off}. The very mechanisms designed to enforce cross-shot visual consistency---such as shared attention and in-context conditioning---inevitably suppress the localized entity control required to execute dynamic character entrances and exits. Consequently, models struggle to comply with the narrative dynamics that define true storytelling.

Current evaluation paradigms systematically overlook this architectural tension. The majority of prominent video generation benchmarks~\cite{vbench,evalcrafter,t2vcompbench} are heavily optimized for measuring single-shot visual quality and cross-frame visual consistency. By relying on global embedding distances or static frame-level similarities, these metrics reduce complex narrative dynamics to simple frame-by-frame image comparisons. Under this paradigm, a dynamic narrative where two characters meet and then part ways receives the same consistency rating as a stagnant video where they remain together throughout. 

The root problem of evaluating narrative dynamics is combinatorial. As the number of shots and characters increases, the possible configurations of character presence and absence grow exponentially. While recent multi-shot benchmarks~\cite{moviebench,locot2v,shotadapter} attempt to evaluate extended horizons, they rely entirely on empirically curated prompt sets. To the best of our knowledge, no existing benchmark provides mathematical assurance that their evaluated scenarios adequately cover this vast combinatorial narrative space. 

We reformulate this problem through the lens of linear algebra. By abstracting a narrative script into a \emph{state transition matrix} that tracks character entrances and exits, we mathematically prove that \textbf{every conceivable multi-shot narrative topology} can be exactly generated from just \textbf{two fundamental algebraic basis patterns} (Relay and Split). Through intuitive narrative operations---namely, character role swapping and time reversal---combined with simple linear combinations, our framework spans the entire narrative space (\textbf{Theorem~\ref{thm:completeness}}). 
To the best of our knowledge, this is the first result that elevates video generation evaluation from heuristic scenario selection to \emph{mathematically complete formal verification}: successfully testing a model on our basis-derived patterns rigorously certifies its capability across all possible narrative structures.

To formalize this mathematically sound evaluation, we contribute the following:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
  \item The \textbf{\diagonal{} framework} (\S\ref{sec:framework}): A theoretical formalization of multi-entity, multi-shot narratives as state transition matrices. We provide a rigorous proof that all possible narrative matrices are spanned by just two basis patterns (\textbf{Theorem~\ref{thm:completeness}}, \S\ref{sec:completeness}), with an extension to arbitrary lengths and cast sizes (\textbf{Theorem~2}, Appendix~B).
  \item The \textbf{\stem{} evaluation pipeline} (\S\ref{sec:stem}): A novel automated evaluation protocol built upon an algebraic \emph{error matrix}. By comparing generated transitions against the prescribed script, we derive density-normalized metrics---Prescribed Transition Match (PTM) and Entity Presence Accuracy (EPA)---to decouple true narrative fidelity from detector noise.
  \item \textbf{MEMS-1K Benchmark} (\S\ref{sec:dataset}): A mathematically verified dataset of 1{,}000 multi-entity, multi-shot scenarios spanning 7 narrative patterns. We evaluate 4{,}000 videos across four distinct architectures, revealing a \textbf{four-type failure taxonomy} that provides structured diagnostics for the coherence--compliance trade-off.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%% ============================================================

\paragraph{Text-to-Video Generation.}
Modern video synthesis rests on diffusion models~\cite{ddpm,ldm,scoreflow}, extended temporally through UNet attention~\cite{svd,imagenvideo,ho2022video}, scalable DiT architectures~\cite{dit,wan,sora} with transformer backbones~\cite{attention,vit}, flow-matching~\cite{flowmatching}, and classifier-free guidance~\cite{cfg}. Open-source systems such as Open-Sora~\cite{opensora} and AnimateDiff~\cite{animatediff} provide general-purpose motion modules, while Lumiere~\cite{lumiere} and Emu Video~\cite{emuvideo} explore space-time generation. Despite these advances in visual fidelity, fine-grained multi-entity narrative control remains an open problem.

\paragraph{Multi-Shot Video Generation.}
Multi-shot extensions address cross-shot coherence through a variety of structural paradigms. Current approaches utilize Consistent Self-Attention (CSA)~\cite{storydiff,consistory}, cross-attention~\cite{videostudio}, latent initialization~\cite{mevg}, LLM-guided layouts~\cite{vgot,videodirectorgpt,storygen}, keyframe transitions~\cite{seine,dynamicrafter}, flow-matching portraits~\cite{echoshot}, IP-Adapter~\cite{ip_adapter}, and in-context LoRA~\cite{vic,lora}. Longer-horizon generation has also been explored through autoregressive token models~\cite{videopoet,phenaki}. While these methods succeed in maintaining subject identity, their varying approaches to temporal conditioning inherently affect how they handle character appearances and disappearances. We evaluate representatives from four distinct paradigms---CSA (StoryDiffusion), flow-matching (EchoShot), in-context DiT (VIC), and cascaded T2I+I2V (VGoT)---providing the first cross-paradigm comparison focused explicitly on narrative dynamics.

\paragraph{Video Generation Benchmarks.}
Existing benchmarks primarily quantify generation quality via embedding similarities and statistical distributions (Table~\ref{tab:benchmark}). Metrics like FID~\cite{fid} and FVD~\cite{fvd} measure distributional fidelity but are agnostic to narrative structure, while CLIPScore~\cite{clipscore} measures text--image alignment purely at the single-frame level. 
Comprehensive evaluation suites such as VBench~\cite{vbench,vbench2} and EvalCrafter~\cite{evalcrafter} compute frame-level CLIP~\cite{clip} or DINO~\cite{dinov2} similarities that conflate subjects with their backgrounds. T2V-CompBench~\cite{t2vcompbench} targets compositional ability but is restricted to single clips. 

Recent benchmarks have begun to recognize the unique challenges of multi-shot storytelling. ShotAdapter~\cite{shotadapter} isolates subject regions but cannot algebraically decompose multi-entity presence across discrete shots. MovieBench~\cite{moviebench} and LoCoT2V~\cite{locot2v} evaluate long-form coherence, yet they lack granular, entity-level transition metrics. Most critically, all these benchmarks rely on empirically curated prompt sets, offering no formal guarantees over the combinatorial narrative space. \diagonal{} complements this line of work by shifting the paradigm from heuristic sampling to formal verification, utilizing state transition matrices to verify models against a mathematically complete space of storytelling topologies.

\section{The \diagonal{} Framework}
\label{sec:framework}
%% ============================================================

We first build the theoretical foundation: formulating narratives as state transition matrices (\S\ref{sec:formalization}), defining their linear operators and algebraic basis (\S\ref{sec:basis}), and rigorously proving the completeness of the narrative space (\S\ref{sec:completeness}). Section~\ref{sec:stem} then operationalizes this theory into a practical evaluation pipeline.

\subsection{Narratives as State Transition Matrices}
\label{sec:formalization}

A multi-shot video consists of $T$ consecutive shots depicting up to $N$ named entities (e.g., ``a tuxedo cat,'' ``a orange tabby''). To mathematically formalize their appearances, we define the binary \emph{presence matrix} $S \in \{0,1\}^{T \times N}$, which records entity visibility across shots: $S_{t,e} = 1$ if entity $e$ appears in shot $t$, and $0$ otherwise.

To evaluate the dynamic storytelling (entrances and exits), we extract the narrative derivative via temporal differencing, yielding the \emph{state transition matrix} $\Delta S$:
\begin{equation}
\Delta S \;=\; S_{2:T} \;-\; S_{1:T\!-\!1}, \qquad \Delta S_{t,e} \in \{-1,\; 0,\; +1\},
\label{eq:delta_s}
\end{equation}
where the values algebraically encode the narrative operations: $+1$ (\emph{enter}), $-1$ (\emph{exit}), and $0$ (\emph{hold}). For example, consider a ``Relay'' narrative ($A \!\to\! AB \!\to\! B$), where entity $B$ enters at transition 1, and entity $A$ exits at transition 2:
\begin{equation}
S = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}
\quad \implies \quad
\Delta S = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & +1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}.
\label{eq:relay_example}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Canonical Embedding.}
For standard 3-shot narratives ($T\!=\!3$), $\Delta S$ inherently has 2 rows representing transitions. To unify the algebraic treatment across varying entity counts (up to $N=3$), we embed all matrices into a canonical form via spatial zero-padding. For instance, the $2\!\times\!2$ Relay matrix extends naturally into the target space $\mathbb{M}_{2 \times 3}(\mathbb{Z})$:
\begin{equation}
\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} 0 & +1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}}_{\text{Relay}~(2\!\times\!2)}
\;\;\longrightarrow\;\;
\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} 0 & +1 & 0 \\ -1 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}}_{\text{Canonical}~(2\!\times\!3)}.
\label{eq:canonical}
\end{equation}
This canonical projection enables a unified completeness proof over a single matrix space. The theoretical extension to arbitrary $T$ and $N$ is provided in Appendix~B (Theorem~2).

\subsection{Linear Operators and the Narrative Basis}
\label{sec:basis}

To systematically span the narrative space, we define two linear operators that manipulate narrative topologies.

\begin{definition}[Character Role Swapping / Column Permutation]
\label{def:col_perm}
Let $\sigma_{ij}$ denote the transposition swapping columns $i$ and $j$. This operator reassigns entity roles without altering the underlying causal topology:
\begin{equation}
(\sigma_{ij} \circ \Delta S)_{t,e} = \Delta S_{t,\,\sigma_{ij}^{-1}(e)}.
\label{eq:col_perm}
\end{equation}
Compositions read left-to-right: $\sigma_{23} \circ \sigma_{12}$ first swaps columns 1 and 2, then 2 and 3.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Time Reversal]
\label{def:time_rev}
The time reversal operator $\calT$ rewinds the narrative by inverting the temporal order and negating all signs, thereby converting entries to exits and vice versa:
\begin{equation}
(\calT \circ \Delta S)_{t,e} = -\Delta S_{(T\!-\!1)-t,\,e}.
\label{eq:time_rev}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Topological Basis Matrices]
\label{def:basis}
We identify two matrices that serve as the fundamental algebraic basis of the narrative space (represented in canonical $2 \times 3$ form):
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
  \item \emph{Relay} ($\calB_1$): A sequential hand-off ($A \!\to\! AB \!\to\! B$). $\calB_1 = \left[\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & +1 & 0 \\ -1 & 0 & 0 \end{smallmatrix}\right]$.
  \item \emph{Split} ($\calB_2$): Co-occurring entities separate ($AB \!\to\! A \!\to\! B$). $\calB_2 = \left[\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & -1 & 0 \\ -1 & +1 & 0 \end{smallmatrix}\right]$.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\paragraph{Derived Narrative Patterns.}
Let $E_{ij}$ denote the standard basis matrix with $1$ at position $(i,j)$ and $0$ elsewhere. Utilizing $\calB_1$, $\calB_2$, the defined operators, and $\mathbb{Z}$-linear combinations, we construct five additional benchmark patterns:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
  \item \textbf{Reverse Relay} ($B \!\to\! AB \!\to\! A$): $\sigma_{12} \circ \calB_1 = \calT \circ \calB_1 = \left[\begin{smallmatrix} +1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 \end{smallmatrix}\right]$.
  \item \textbf{Convergence} ($A \!\to\! B \!\to\! AB$): $\calT \circ (\sigma_{12} \circ \calB_2) = \left[\begin{smallmatrix} -1 & +1 & 0 \\ +1 & 0 & 0 \end{smallmatrix}\right]$.
  \item \textbf{Accumulation} ($A \!\to\! AB \!\to\! ABC$): $E_{12} + E_{23} = \left[\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & +1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & +1 \end{smallmatrix}\right]$. Introduces a third entity dynamically.
  \item \textbf{Reduction} ($ABC \!\to\! AB \!\to\! A$): $\calT \circ (\text{Accumulation}) = \left[\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & 0 & -1 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 \end{smallmatrix}\right]$.
  \item \textbf{Sliding Window} ($AB \!\to\! BC \!\to\! C$): $-E_{11} + E_{13} - E_{22} = \left[\begin{smallmatrix} -1 & 0 & +1 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 \end{smallmatrix}\right]$. Involves all three entities across both transitions.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Completeness Theorem}
\label{sec:completeness}

We now prove that the \diagonal{} framework mathematically covers all possible scenarios.

\begin{theorem}[Completeness of the Narrative Space]
\label{thm:completeness}
Under character role swapping (column permutation $\sigma \in \Sigma_3$) and integer-coefficient linear combination, the two basis patterns $\calB_1$ (Relay) and $\calB_2$ (Split) span the entire state transition matrix space $\mathbb{M}_{2 \times 3}(\mathbb{Z})$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
To prove this, it is sufficient to construct all six standard basis matrices $E_{ij}$ from $\calB_1$ and $\calB_2$, as any integer matrix is inherently a $\mathbb{Z}$-linear combination of $\{E_{ij}\}$.

\emph{Step 1: Isolate $E_{11} - E_{21}$.}\;
Consider the following two column-permuted variants of $\calB_1$:
\begin{equation}
\sigma_{13} \circ \calB_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & +1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}, \quad
\sigma_{23} \circ \sigma_{12} \circ \calB_1 = \begin{bmatrix} +1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}.
\label{eq:two_perms}
\end{equation}
Taking the specific linear combination $\calB_1 - (\sigma_{13} \circ \calB_1) + (\sigma_{23} \circ \sigma_{12} \circ \calB_1)$ yields:
\begin{align}
&\begin{bmatrix} 0 & +1 & 0 \\ -1 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}
- \begin{bmatrix} 0 & +1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}
+ \begin{bmatrix} +1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \notag \\[3pt]
&\;= \begin{bmatrix} +1 & 0 & 0 \\ -1 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}
\;= E_{11} - E_{21}. \label{eq:e11_e21}
\end{align}

\emph{Step 2: Extract the standard basis $\{E_{ij}\}$.}\;
By adding a permuted Split matrix ($\sigma_{12} \circ \calB_2 = \left[\begin{smallmatrix} -1 & 0 & 0 \\ +1 & -1 & 0 \end{smallmatrix}\right]$) to Eq.~\ref{eq:e11_e21}, we perfectly isolate $-E_{22}$:
\begin{align}
(E_{11} - E_{21}) + \sigma_{12} \circ \calB_2
&= \begin{bmatrix} +1 & 0 & 0 \\ -1 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}
 + \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 & 0 \\ +1 & -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \notag \\[3pt]
&= \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} = -E_{22}. \label{eq:isolate_e22}
\end{align}
Multiplying by $-1$ yields $E_{22}$. Applying column permutations to $E_{22}$ generates the remaining row-2 matrices: $E_{21} = \sigma_{12} \circ E_{22}$ and $E_{23} = \sigma_{23} \circ E_{22}$. Next, adding the extracted $E_{21}$ back to Eq.~\ref{eq:e11_e21} isolates $E_{11}$ since $(E_{11} - E_{21}) + E_{21} = E_{11}$. Subsequent column permutations of $E_{11}$ produce $E_{12} = \sigma_{12} \circ E_{11}$ and $E_{13} = \sigma_{13} \circ E_{11}$, giving:
\begin{equation}
\{E_{11},\, E_{12},\, E_{13},\, E_{21},\, E_{22},\, E_{23}\} \;\subset\; \operatorname{span}_{\mathbb{Z}}\!\bigl(\calB_1,\, \calB_2,\, \Sigma_3\bigr). \qedhere
\label{eq:all_eij}
\end{equation}
\end{proof}

\noindent A step-by-step derivation including all intermediate matrices is provided in Appendix~A. Crucially, all column permutations of $\calB_1$ share an invariant row-sum constraint (row 1 sums to $+1$, row 2 sums to $-1$). Therefore, $\calB_2$ is mathematically essential to break this symmetry; no combination of $\calB_1$ variants alone can span the full space.

\subsection{Theoretical \& Practical Implications}
\label{sec:implications}

\paragraph{Mathematically Complete Verification.}
Because the 7 benchmark patterns are derived from the foundational bases $\calB_1$ and $\calB_2$, \diagonal{} forms a \emph{mathematically complete} evaluation suite. Any complex narrative $M \in \mathbb{M}_{2 \times 3}(\mathbb{Z})$ can be expressed as:
\begin{equation}
M = \sum_{i,j} \alpha_{ij}\, E_{ij}, \quad \alpha_{ij} \in \mathbb{Z}.
\label{eq:completeness_expansion}
\end{equation}
Consequently, rigorously testing a generative model on these foundational patterns structurally certifies its capability to execute \emph{any} conceivable narrative topology. This paradigm uniquely distinguishes \diagonal{} from prior evaluation methods~\cite{moviebench,locot2v,shotadapter}, which sample from the narrative space without guarantees of completeness.

\paragraph{Scalability.}
For continuous evaluation of longer, $T$-shot videos involving $N$ entities, the target algebraic space $\mathbb{M}_{(T-1) \times N}(\mathbb{Z})$ has a dimension of $2(T\!-\!1)N$. Importantly, this high-dimensional space can still be completely spanned by strategically zero-padding and temporally shifting just $2(T\!-\!1)$ basis matrices. This ensures that narrative evaluation scales linearly, completely bypassing the combinatorial explosion of empirical prompting (proved in Theorem~2, Appendix~B).

\section{The \stem{} Evaluation Methodology}
\label{sec:stem}
%% ============================================================

To operationalize the theoretical completeness of the \diagonal{} framework, we introduce \stem{} (State Transition Error Matching), a fully automated evaluation pipeline. \stem{} bridges the gap between the continuous visual space and our discrete algebraic framework by employing Vision-Language Models (VLMs) as state detectors, ultimately yielding density-normalized $\ell_0$-norm metrics.

\subsection{VLM-Based State Detection}
\label{sec:vlm_instantiation}

Evaluating a generated video requires projecting its continuous visual space into the discrete observed presence matrix $S_\text{obs} \in \{0,1\}^{T \times N}$. We accomplish this by extracting the middle frame of each shot $t \in \{1, \ldots, T\}$ and querying a VLM with a binary question for each prescribed entity $e$:
\begin{equation}
S_\text{obs}[t, e] = \mathbb{1}\!\bigl[\text{VLM}(\text{frame}_t,\;\text{``Is entity } e \text{ visible?''}) = \text{Yes}\bigr].
\label{eq:vlm_probe}
\end{equation}
We utilize Qwen2-VL-7B-Instruct~\cite{qwen2vl} for this detection. Formulating detection as discrete binary queries circumvents the limitations of continuous contrastive probes (e.g., CLIP similarity), which inherently suffer from bag-of-words effects and exhibit marginal score gaps between entity presence and absence (Cohen's $d \approx 0.23$--$0.59$). Such continuous probes often require fragile, dataset-specific threshold tuning, making them unsuitable for exact narrative formalization.

\subsection{Algebraic Error Matrix and Failure Taxonomy}
\label{sec:error_taxonomy}

Given $S_\text{obs}$, we compute the observed state transition matrix $\Delta S_\text{obs} = S_{\text{obs},\,2:T} - S_{\text{obs},\,1:T\!-\!1}$. The cornerstone of our diagnostic pipeline is the \emph{error matrix}, which captures all narrative deviations within a single algebraic object.

\begin{definition}[Error Matrix]
\label{def:error_matrix}
Let $\Delta S^*$ be the prescribed transition matrix. The error matrix $E \in \{-2, -1, 0, +1, +2\}^{(T-1) \times N}$ is defined as:
\begin{equation}
E \;=\; \Delta S_\text{obs} \;-\; \Delta S^*.
\label{eq:error_matrix}
\end{equation}
A perfect narrative execution yields $E = \mathbf{0}$. Any non-zero entry uniquely localizes a narrative failure to a specific transition--entity pair $(t, e)$.
\end{definition}

The algebraic structure of $E$ naturally induces an exhaustive taxonomy of narrative failures (Fig.~\ref{fig:overview}(c)).

\begin{definition}[Failure Taxonomy]
\label{def:failure_taxonomy}
Based on the joint state of $(E_{t,e},\, \Delta S^*_{t,e})$, we classify transition failures into four distinct types:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
  \item \textbf{Type~I} (Context Bleeding): $\Delta S^*_{t,e} = -1 \;\land\; E_{t,e} > 0$. An entity anomalously persists despite a prescribed exit.
  \item \textbf{Type~II} (Amnesia): $\Delta S^*_{t,e} = +1 \;\land\; E_{t,e} < 0$. An entity fails to appear at its prescribed entry.
  \item \textbf{Type~III} (Spurious Transition): $\Delta S^*_{t,e} = 0 \;\land\; E_{t,e} \neq 0$. An unintended state change occurs during a prescribed hold phase.
  \item \textbf{Type~IV} (Reversal): $|E_{t,e}| = 2$. The observed transition is diametrically opposite to the prescribed action (e.g., exiting instead of entering).
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\noindent\emph{Note.}\; Type~IV represents the most severe compounded failure and is algebraically a strict subset of Types~I and II: a single $|E_{t,e}| = 2$ entry simultaneously satisfies the condition for its parent type and for Type~IV. The failure counts in Table~\ref{tab:failures} report each type independently.

\subsection{Density-Normalized $\ell_0$-Norm Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

A naive aggregation of $E$ would compute its Frobenius norm $\lVert E \rVert_F$. However, because hold cells ($\Delta S^*_{t,e} = 0$) empirically outnumber active transitions by a factor of 2 to 4 in standard narratives, VLM detection noise in these static regions disproportionately inflates the unnormalized distance. This effectively drowns the true narrative signal ($\lVert E \rVert_F$ achieves only 1 of 6 pairwise significances at $\alpha = 0.05$; see Supplementary, Frobenius Distance Analysis). To rigorously isolate narrative fidelity, we decompose the evaluation into two complementary $\ell_0$-norm metrics.

\begin{definition}[Prescribed Transition Match, PTM]
\label{def:ptm}
PTM quantifies \emph{microscopic transition fidelity} by evaluating only the prescribed dynamic changes. Let $\mathcal{M} \in \{0,1\}^{(T-1) \times N}$ be the binary prescription mask where $\mathcal{M}_{t,e} = \mathbb{1}[\Delta S^*_{t,e} \neq 0]$. Then:
\begin{equation}
\text{PTM} = 1 - \frac{\lVert E \odot \mathcal{M} \rVert_0}{\lVert \mathcal{M} \rVert_0}
\label{eq:ptm}
\end{equation}
where $\odot$ denotes the Hadamard product, $\lVert \cdot \rVert_0$ counts non-zero entries, and $\lVert \mathcal{M} \rVert_0$ is the total number of prescribed transitions.
\end{definition}

\noindent By masking out the hold cells, PTM elegantly eliminates the dominant source of VLM noise while capturing the exact operations that define storytelling.

\begin{definition}[Entity Presence Accuracy, EPA]
\label{def:epa}
EPA assesses \emph{macroscopic cast compliance} by computing the element-wise accuracy of the underlying presence states:
\begin{equation}
\text{EPA} = 1 - \frac{\lVert S_\text{obs} - S^* \rVert_0}{T \cdot N}
\label{eq:epa}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\noindent Together, PTM (``Did the right \emph{transitions} happen?'') and EPA (``Are the right \emph{entities} present?'') provide a mathematically decoupled, density-normalized evaluation of multi-shot video generation---counting discrete mismatches via $\ell_0$-norms in complementary matrix subspaces.

\paragraph{Structured Prompt Generation.}
We systematically generate all scenarios using Gemini~1.5~\cite{gemini} via strict structural templates. \paragraph{Semantic Diversity \& Disentanglement.}
The selected entities span humans, animals, vehicles, food items, and everyday objects, while scenes cover indoor, outdoor, urban, rural, fantasy, and professional environments. Each pattern is instantiated with unique entity--scene combinations to prevent models from exploiting surface-level shortcuts. Crucially, to prevent VLM detection ambiguity (e.g., confusing ``a dog'' with ``a wolf''), we rigorously filter the entity pairings. The finalized pairs exhibit a low mean CLIP text similarity of 0.23 ($\sigma = 0.08$), and any pairs with a similarity score exceeding 0.45 are strictly excluded to ensure distinct visual disentanglement.

\subsection{Metric Sensitivity Across Patterns}
\label{sec:pattern_analysis}

The per-pattern breakdown (Table~\ref{tab:main}) reveals how \stem{} exposes architectural limitations that remain invisible to aggregate scalar scores.

\paragraph{Pattern Difficulty Gradient.}
The PTM spread (best minus worst model) varies significantly, from a narrow 0.11 on simple patterns (Relay, Reverse Relay) to a wide 0.28 on complex ones (Sliding Window). This confirms that 3-transition patterns (Split, Convergence, Sliding Window) provide much stronger metric discrimination than simpler 2-transition structures.

\paragraph{Exposing Architectural Bottlenecks.}
StoryDiffusion's Consistent Self-Attention (CSA) excels at complex multi-entity transitions (e.g., Convergence: 0.277). Conversely, VGoT's cascaded pipeline entirely collapses on Convergence (PTM = 0.053), explicitly revealing that its rendering backbone (DynamiCrafter) struggles to introduce new entities on demand if they were absent from the initial layout. Meanwhile, VIC shows a near-uniform failure across all patterns (PTM range $\approx$ 0.032), confirming that heavy in-context conditioning creates a fixed "coherence ceiling" that severely stifles narrative flexibility.

\subsection{Error Taxonomy Analysis}
\label{sec:failure_analysis}

Fewer than 1.5\% of videos are entirely failure-free, even for the best-performing model (Table~\ref{tab:failures}). This stark statistic underscores that multi-entity narrative execution remains an unsolved challenge. \stem{}'s error taxonomy moves beyond this aggregate failure rate to provide actionable architectural diagnostics (Fig.~\ref{fig:qualitative}).

\paragraph{Context Bleeding (Type I) in In-Context Models.}
VIC suffers the highest rate of Context Bleeding (75.6\%). Because its in-context conditioning heavily anchors the generation on prior frames, the model actively resists removing previously established entities, causing their visual context to inappropriately "bleed" into subsequent shots.

\paragraph{Amnesia (Type II) in Cascaded Pipelines.}
VGoT exhibits the highest Amnesia rate (82.1%). This highlights a critical planning-rendering bottleneck: if the initial text-to-image layout fails to firmly establish an entity, the subsequent video motion module remains permanently "amnesic" to the prompt, completely missing the prescribed entrance.

\paragraph{Spurious Transitions (Type III) via Noisy Exploration.}
StoryDiffusion records the highest rate of spurious transitions (51.4%). We attribute this to the "noisy exploration" inherent in CSA's feature-sharing mechanism, which occasionally hallucinates unprompted changes. Paradoxically, this exploratory flexibility prevents the model from freezing the narrative, allowing it to successfully execute intended transitions more often than its peers (hence its high PTM).

\paragraph{Reversal (Type IV) as a Universal Blind Spot.}
Although relatively rare (7.6--13.9%), Reversals—where the observed transition is diametrically opposite to the prescribed one—occur across all models. As a compounded failure (where $|E_{t,e}| = 2$), its persistence proves a profound shared blind spot: current architectures lack a fundamental understanding of the directional causality (entrance vs. exit) that drives storytelling.

\subsection{Human Evaluation}
\label{sec:human_eval}

To validate our automated metrics against human perception, 15 expert evaluators (computer science researchers with video generation experience) assessed 40 randomly sampled scenarios. Evaluators conducted pairwise comparisons of generated videos alongside the original per-shot text prompts, rating \emph{Narrative Compliance} on a 5-point Likert scale (120 total comparisons).

The automated PTM rankings strongly correlate with human narrative judgments ($\rho = 0.80$, $p < 0.05$; Fleiss' $\kappa = 0.67$), validating PTM as a reliable proxy for human perception. Notably, VIC ranked last in human preference (17.6\% win rate). Furthermore, the high rate of "Ties" (42.3%) in human evaluations independently corroborates our automated finding: current models frequently experience mutual failure, where neither model successfully executes the prescribed narrative transitions.

\subsection{Discussion \& Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{Discriminative Validity over Absolute Accuracy.}
While VLM detection is inherently noisy (achieving 52\% TPR and 67\% TNR), this noise is distributed \emph{systematically} across all evaluated methods. For a benchmark, the critical requirement is \emph{discriminative validity}, not absolute accuracy. \stem{} achieves this resoundingly: the Kruskal-Wallis test ($H = 302.7$), non-overlapping CIs, and massive effect sizes (up to Cohen's $d = 0.72$) prove its robustness. We also evaluated Grounding DINO~\cite{groundingdino} (TNR $= 4\%$) and smaller 2B VLMs (TPR $= 40\%$), confirming that the 7B Instruct VLM provides the optimal balance of detection capability (see Supplementary, Evaluation Details).

\paragraph{Empirical Validation of Metric Decomposition.}
As theorized in Section~\ref{sec:metrics}, a naive aggregation of the error matrix via the Frobenius norm ($\|E\|_F$) fails empirically. Because static hold cells ($\Delta S^* = 0$) naturally outnumber active transitions by a factor of 2 to 4, VLM detection noise in these static regions completely drowns out the dynamic narrative signal. Indeed, $\|E\|_F$ achieves statistical significance in only 1 of 6 pairwise model comparisons (at $\alpha = 0.05$). The $\ell_0$-norm decomposition into PTM and EPA is therefore not merely a theoretical choice, but an empirical necessity to isolate true storytelling fidelity from background detector noise.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ============================================================

In this work, we introduced the \diagonal{} framework, fundamentally shifting the evaluation of multi-shot video generation from heuristic visual similarities to mathematically rigorous formal verification. By abstracting narratives into state transition matrices, we proved that the entire combinatorial space of multi-entity storytelling can be perfectly spanned by just two algebraic basis patterns (Relay and Split). 

To operationalize this theory, we developed the \stem{} evaluation pipeline, which decouples true narrative fidelity from underlying detector noise via density-normalized metrics. Our extensive evaluation on the MEMS-1K benchmark definitively exposes the \emph{coherence--compliance trade-off} across four major generative paradigms. With stark statistical significance ($H = 302.7$, $p < 10^{-65}$) and the revelation that fewer than 1.5\% of generated videos are entirely error-free, our four-type algebraic failure taxonomy provides the community with a clear, actionable diagnostic tool to overcome current architectural blind spots.

\paragraph{Future Directions.}
The severe failure rates across all evaluated models indicate that current architectures cannot natively resolve the coherence--compliance trade-off. We hypothesize that future breakthroughs will require explicit \emph{entity-aware} conditioning mechanisms---such as dynamic entity-specific attention masks~\cite{flexattention}, grounded spatial generation~\cite{gligen}, or per-entity guidance scales---to achieve precise narrative compliance without sacrificing cross-shot visual consistency.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
While the theoretical completeness of the \diagonal{} framework scales to arbitrary $T$ shots and $N$ entities (Theorem~2, Appendix~B), our empirical benchmark (MEMS-1K) currently focuses on the foundational $3$-shot, $\leq\!3$-entity sequences. Furthermore, while our $\ell_0$-norm decomposition successfully isolates the narrative signal, the underlying VLM detector (52\% TPR) introduces systematic noise. As foundational VLMs continue to evolve, the absolute accuracy of the \stem{} pipeline will naturally improve without requiring structural changes to our metrics.

\paragraph{Reproducibility.}
To facilitate future research and benchmark standardizations, we release all generation prompts, the complete \stem{} evaluation codebase, and the 4{,}000 generated videos utilized in our experiments.
