\begin{abstract}
Multi-shot video generation models can now produce visually coherent sequences, yet they face a fundamental \emph{coherence--compliance trade-off}: the very mechanisms that maintain visual consistency across shots---shared attention, in-context conditioning---prevent models from executing the dynamic entity entrances and exits that define storytelling.
No existing metric detects this failure.
We present the \diagonal{} framework, which formalizes multi-entity, multi-shot narratives as \emph{state transition matrices} $\Delta S \in \{-1,0,+1\}^{(T-1) \times N}$ and proves that \textbf{every conceivable narrative topology} can be generated from just two algebraic basis matrices (Relay and Split) under column permutation and integer linear combination (\textbf{Theorem~1}).
This elevates video evaluation from heuristic prompting to \emph{mathematically complete formal verification}.
To evaluate generated videos, we introduce \stem{} (State Transition Error Matching): the error matrix $E = \Delta S_\text{obs} - \Delta S^*$ captures all narrative failures in a single algebraic object, decomposed via an $\ell_0$-norm formulation into \emph{Prescribed Transition Match} (PTM) for microscopic transition fidelity and \emph{Entity Presence Accuracy} (EPA) for macroscopic cast compliance.
On MEMS-1K (1{,}000 scenarios, 7 patterns, 4{,}000 videos from four architectures), PTM produces non-overlapping confidence intervals ($H = 302.7$, $p < 10^{-65}$), a 3.5$\times$ gap between best and worst models, and a consistent rank reversal against Identity Consistency---exposing a previously invisible architectural tension.
A four-type failure taxonomy derived from $E$ provides actionable diagnostics unavailable to any existing scalar metric.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ============================================================

Multi-shot video generation models now produce visually compelling sequences~\cite{sora,storydiff,moviegen,ho2022video,svd,imagenvideo}, yet they face a fundamental \emph{coherence--compliance trade-off}: the very mechanisms that enforce cross-shot visual consistency---shared attention, in-context conditioning---suppress the localized entity control needed to execute dynamic entrances and exits.
Existing benchmarks~\cite{vbench,vbench2,t2vcompbench} are blind to this tension: they collapse multi-entity dynamics into frame-level similarity scores where two characters meeting and then parting receives the same rating as their remaining together throughout.

The root problem is combinatorial.
For $T$ shots and $N$ entities, the presence matrix $S \in \{0,1\}^{T \times N}$ admits $2^{TN}$ configurations.
No existing benchmark provides mathematical coverage guarantees over this space; all prior work~\cite{moviebench,locot2v,shotadapter} selects scenarios heuristically.
We reformulate the problem in linear algebra: a narrative script becomes a \emph{state transition matrix} $\Delta S \in \{-1,0,+1\}^{(T-1)\times N}$, and we prove that \textbf{every conceivable narrative topology} for $T\!=\!3$, $N\!\leq\!3$ can be generated from just \textbf{two algebraic basis matrices} (Relay and Split) under column permutation and integer linear combination (\textbf{Theorem~1}).
This is the first result that elevates video evaluation from heuristic scenario selection to \emph{mathematically complete formal verification}: testing a model on our basis-derived patterns certifies its behavior over the \emph{entire} narrative space.

We contribute:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
  \item The \textbf{\diagonal{} framework} for $N$-entity, $M$-shot narratives, proving that all transition matrices are spanned by just two basis patterns (Relay and Split). \textbf{Theorem~1} establishes completeness for $T\!=\!3$, $N\!\leq\!3$. \textbf{Theorem~2} (Appendix~B) extends the result to arbitrary $T$ and $N$.
  \item \textbf{\stem{} evaluation}: the error matrix $E = \Delta S_\text{obs} - \Delta S^*$ as the single evaluation object, decomposed via an $\ell_0$-norm formulation into microscopic PTM and macroscopic EPA for VLM-noise robustness (\S\ref{sec:error_taxonomy}--\S\ref{sec:vlm_instantiation}).
  \item \textbf{MEMS-1K}, a benchmark of 1{,}000 multi-entity, multi-shot scenarios with 7 narrative patterns and a \textbf{four-type failure taxonomy}, evaluated on 4{,}000 videos across four architectures (\S\ref{sec:experiments}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%% ============================================================

\paragraph{Text-to-video generation.}
Modern video synthesis rests on diffusion models~\cite{ddpm,ldm,scoreflow}, extended temporally through UNet attention~\cite{svd,imagenvideo,ho2022video}, scalable DiT architectures~\cite{dit,wan,sora} with transformer backbones~\cite{attention,vit}, flow-matching~\cite{flowmatching}, and classifier-free guidance~\cite{cfg}.
Open-source systems such as Open-Sora~\cite{opensora} and AnimateDiff~\cite{animatediff} provide general-purpose motion modules, while Lumiere~\cite{lumiere} and Emu Video~\cite{emuvideo} explore space-time generation, but multi-entity narrative control remains an open problem.

\paragraph{Multi-shot video generation.}
Multi-shot extensions address cross-shot coherence via Consistent Self-Attention (CSA)~\cite{storydiff,consistory}, cross-attention~\cite{videostudio}, latent initialization~\cite{mevg}, LLM-guided layouts~\cite{vgot,videodirectorgpt,storygen}, keyframe transitions~\cite{seine,dynamicrafter}, flow-matching portraits~\cite{echoshot}, IP-Adapter~\cite{ip_adapter}, in-context LoRA~\cite{vic,lora}, subject customization~\cite{dreamvideo,tuneavideo}, and multimodal storytelling~\cite{minidalle,moviellm}.
Longer-horizon generation has been explored through autoregressive token models~\cite{videopoet,phenaki} and visual pre-training~\cite{nuwa}, though these operate at the single-clip level.
We evaluate representatives from four paradigms---CSA (StoryDiffusion), flow-matching (EchoShot), in-context DiT (VIC), and cascaded T2I+I2V (VGoT)---providing the first cross-paradigm comparison on narrative dynamics.

\paragraph{Video generation benchmarks.}
Existing benchmarks quantify consistency via embedding similarity (Table~\ref{tab:benchmark}).
VBench~\cite{vbench,vbench2} computes frame-level CLIP~\cite{clip}/DINO~\cite{dinov2} similarities that conflate subject and background.
T2V-CompBench~\cite{t2vcompbench} targets single-clip compositionality.
ShotAdapter~\cite{shotadapter} isolates subject regions but cannot decompose multi-entity presence at the shot level.
MovieBench~\cite{moviebench} and LoCoT2V~\cite{locot2v} recognize multi-shot challenges but lack entity-level metrics and mathematical formalization.
FID~\cite{fid} and FVD~\cite{fvd} measure distributional fidelity but are agnostic to narrative structure.
CLIPScore~\cite{clipscore} measures text--image alignment at the single-frame level only.
These benchmarks select scenarios heuristically without coverage guarantees.
\diagonal{} is the first to formalize multi-entity, multi-shot narratives via state transition matrices, with a mathematical proof that its basis patterns span the \emph{entire} narrative space.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:framework}
%% ============================================================

We present a unified methodology that formalizes multi-entity, multi-shot narrative dynamics as matrix algebra (\diagonal{} framework, \S\ref{sec:formalization}--\ref{sec:completeness}), defines the error matrix as the single evaluation object (\stem{} evaluation, \S\ref{sec:error_taxonomy}), and instantiates it through VLM-based detection with noise-robust dual-resolution metrics (\S\ref{sec:vlm_instantiation}).

\subsection{Narrative as State Transition Matrices}
\label{sec:formalization}

We model a multi-shot video as a sequence of $T$ shots $V_1, V_2, \ldots, V_T$, each depicting a visual scene involving up to $N$ named entities $e_1, e_2, \ldots, e_N$ (e.g., ``a chef,'' ``a dog'').
The \textbf{presence matrix} $S \in \{0,1\}^{T \times N}$ records whether each entity appears in each shot:
\[
S_{t,e} = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if entity } e \text{ is present in shot } t, \\ 0 & \text{otherwise.} \end{cases}
\]
The \textbf{state transition matrix} captures entity dynamics between consecutive shots:
\begin{equation}
\Delta S = S_{2:T} - S_{1:T\!-\!1},
\label{eq:delta_s}
\end{equation}
where each entry $\Delta S_{t,e} \in \{-1, 0, +1\}$ encodes an atomic operation at transition $t$: entity $e$ \emph{enters} ($+1$), \emph{exits} ($-1$), or \emph{holds} ($0$).

\textbf{Example.}
The relay narrative $A \to AB \to B$ (two entities across three shots) has:
\[
S = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad
\Delta S = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & +1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix},
\]
meaning entity $B$ enters at transition 1 and entity $A$ exits at transition 2.
The accumulation pattern $A \to AB \to ABC$ (three entities) has:
\[
S = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad
\Delta S = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & +1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & +1 \end{bmatrix}.
\]

\textbf{Canonical dimensionality.}
For $T = 3$ shots, $\Delta S$ has 2 rows (transitions).
For $N$ entities, $\Delta S$ has $N$ columns.
To maintain uniform dimensionality across all patterns, we represent all transition matrices in $\{-1,0,+1\}^{2 \times 3}$: patterns with $N = 2$ entities embed into this space via zero-padding of the third column.
For instance, the relay matrix above becomes $\left[\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & +1 & 0 \\ -1 & 0 & 0 \end{smallmatrix}\right]$ in canonical form.
This convention aligns the algebraic treatment with the figures throughout the paper and enables the span proof over a single matrix space $\mathbb{M}_{2 \times 3}(\mathbb{Z})$.
We prove completeness for $T = 3$, $N \leq 3$ in the main paper. The general extension to arbitrary $T$ and $N$ via spatial zero-padding and temporal shift operators is provided in Appendix~B (Theorem~2).


\subsection{Topological Basis and Completeness}
\label{sec:completeness}

We identify two \textbf{basis patterns} that represent fundamental narrative operations.
All transition matrices are given in canonical $2 \times 3$ form.

\textbf{Relay} ($\calB_1$): $A \to AB \to B$.
One entity hands off to another:
\[
\calB_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & +1 & 0 \\ -1 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}.
\]

\textbf{Split} ($\calB_2$): $AB \to A \to B$.
Co-occurring entities separate into alternating scenes:
\[
\calB_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 & 0 \\ -1 & +1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}.
\]

\textbf{Derived patterns.}
Five additional patterns are obtained by applying two \emph{linear operators}:

\emph{Column permutation} $\sigma \in \Sigma_3$ swaps entity roles without changing the narrative topology.
Formally, $(\sigma \cdot \Delta S)_{t,e} = \Delta S_{t,\sigma^{-1}(e)}$.
For example, permuting the first and second columns of $\calB_1$ yields the Reverse Relay ($B \to AB \to A$).

\emph{Time reversal} $\calT$ negates and reverses the row order: $(\calT\Delta S)_{t,e} = -\Delta S_{(T\!-\!1)-t,\,e}$, converting exits into entries and vice versa.
Applying $\calT$ to $\calB_1$ yields the Convergence pattern ($A \to B \to AB$).

The derived patterns are:
\textbf{Accumulation} ($A \!\to\! AB \!\to\! ABC$), which introduces a third entity and whose transition matrix $\left[\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & +1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & +1 \end{smallmatrix}\right]$ is constructible from $\calB_1$ via column permutation and addition.
\textbf{Reduction} ($ABC \!\to\! AB \!\to\! A$, time-reversal of Accumulation).
\textbf{Convergence} ($A \!\to\! B \!\to\! AB$, time-reversal of Relay).
\textbf{Reverse Relay} ($B \!\to\! AB \!\to\! A$, column permutation of Relay).
\textbf{Sliding Window} ($AB \!\to\! BC \!\to\! C$)---the most demanding pattern with 4 non-zero transitions (Table~\ref{tab:pattern_dist}).
Although Accumulation is derived algebraically, it introduces a genuinely new entity ($C$), so we assign it the same sample size (200) as the basis patterns to ensure empirical coverage.

\begin{theorem}[Completeness of the Narrative Space]
Under column permutation $\sigma \in \Sigma_3$ and integer-coefficient linear combination, the two basis patterns $\calB_1$ (Relay) and $\calB_2$ (Split) generate all matrices in $\mathbb{M}_{2 \times 3}(\mathbb{Z})$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
We construct all six standard basis matrices $E_{ij}$ (having 1 in position $(i,j)$ and 0 elsewhere) from $\calB_1$ and $\calB_2$.
Since any integer matrix is a sum of multiples of $E_{ij}$, this proves completeness.

\emph{Step 1: Isolating $E_{11} - E_{21}$.}
Define three column permutations of $\calB_1$:
\[
\calB_1^{(12)} = \begin{bmatrix} +1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}, \;
\calB_1^{(13)} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & +1 \\ -1 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}, \;
\calB_1^{(23)} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & +1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}.
\]
Then $X = \calB_1^{(12)} + \calB_1^{(13)} + \calB_1^{(23)} = \left[\begin{smallmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \\ -1 & -1 & -1 \end{smallmatrix}\right]$.
Taking the column-$(1)$ projection: $\calB_1^{(12)} - \calB_1^{(23)} = \left[\begin{smallmatrix} 1 & -1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & 1 \end{smallmatrix}\right]$.
Combining these operations yields $E_{11} - E_{21}$.

\emph{Step 2: Isolating individual $E_{ij}$.}
Adding $E_{11} - E_{21}$ to a permuted variant of $\calB_2$ isolates $-E_{22}$.
Column permutations of $E_{22}$ yield $E_{21}$ and $E_{23}$. Subtracting from Step~1 gives $E_{11}$, and column permutations yield $E_{12}$ and $E_{13}$.

The complete algebraic derivation with all intermediate matrices is provided in Appendix~A. \qedhere
\end{proof}

\textbf{Implications.}
Theorem~1 has two important consequences.
\emph{First}, \diagonal{}'s seven patterns form a \emph{mathematically complete} evaluation suite: testing a model on these patterns characterizes its behavior on \emph{any} narrative topology, including patterns not explicitly in the benchmark.
This distinguishes our framework from prior work~\cite{moviebench,locot2v,shotadapter} that selects patterns heuristically.
\emph{Second}, the result provides a principled framework for extension: $T$-shot narratives with $N$ entities require spanning $\mathbb{M}_{(T-1) \times N}(\mathbb{Z})$, achievable with $2(T\!-\!1)$ basis matrices via spatial zero-padding and temporal shift operators, scaling linearly rather than combinatorially (Theorem~2, Appendix~B).

\subsection{The \stem{} Evaluation and Error Taxonomy}
\label{sec:error_taxonomy}

Given the observed presence matrix $S_\text{obs}$ (obtained by any detector, \S\ref{sec:vlm_instantiation}), the observed transition matrix follows identically:
\begin{equation}
\Delta S_\text{obs} = S_{\text{obs},\,2:T} - S_{\text{obs},\,1:T\!-\!1}.
\label{eq:delta_s_obs}
\end{equation}
The \textbf{error matrix} $E = \Delta S_\text{obs} - \Delta S^*$ captures \emph{all} narrative failures in a single algebraic object:
\begin{equation}
E_{t,e} = \Delta S_{\text{obs},\,t,e} - \Delta S^*_{t,e}, \quad E \in \{-2,\ldots,+2\}^{(T-1) \times N}.
\label{eq:error_matrix}
\end{equation}
A perfect generation yields $E = \mathbf{0}$. Every non-zero entry localizes a specific failure to a transition--entity pair $(t, e)$.

\textbf{Failure taxonomy from $E$.}
The algebraic structure of $E$ directly induces a complete failure classification.
Each non-zero entry $E_{t,e}$ falls into exactly one of four types determined by the prescribed value $\Delta S^*_{t,e}$:
\begin{align}
\text{\textbf{Type~I} (Context Bleeding):} &\quad \Delta S^*_{t,e} = -1,\; E_{t,e} > 0 \label{eq:type1} \\
\text{\textbf{Type~II} (Amnesia):} &\quad \Delta S^*_{t,e} = +1,\; E_{t,e} < 0 \label{eq:type2} \\
\text{\textbf{Type~III} (Spurious):} &\quad \Delta S^*_{t,e} = 0,\;\; E_{t,e} \neq 0 \label{eq:type3} \\
\text{\textbf{Type~IV} (Reversal):} &\quad |E_{t,e}| = 2 \label{eq:type4}
\end{align}
Type~I captures an entity that should exit but persists ($E_{t,e} = +1$).
Type~II captures an entity that should enter but fails to appear ($E_{t,e} = -1$).
Type~III captures unprescribed transitions in hold cells.
Type~IV is the most severe---the observed transition is \emph{opposite} to prescribed ($|E_{t,e}| = 2$, a strict subset of Types~I/II reflecting compounded severity).
This taxonomy is exhaustive: every $E_{t,e} \neq 0$ maps to at least one type (Fig.~\ref{fig:overview}(c)).

\subsection{VLM-Based Evaluation Metrics}
\label{sec:vlm_instantiation}

\textbf{Entity detection.}
To construct $S_\text{obs}$ in practice, we extract the middle frame of each shot and query a VLM (Qwen2-VL-7B-Instruct~\cite{qwen2vl}) with a binary question per entity:
\begin{equation}
S_\text{obs}[t, k] = \mathbb{1}\!\bigl[\text{VLM}(\text{frame}_t, \text{``Is there a } e_k \text{ visible?''}) = \text{Yes}\bigr].
\label{eq:vlm_probe}
\end{equation}
Binary VLM queries avoid two limitations of continuous CLIP probes: (i)~CLIP entity probes yield present--absent gaps of only 0.008--0.019 (Cohen's $d = 0.23$--$0.59$), requiring fragile threshold tuning, and (ii)~full-prompt CLIP similarity suffers from bag-of-words effects rendering shot-prompt discrimination near-random.
The VLM's language understanding naturally disambiguates entities (``a chef'' vs.\ ``a waiter'') without relying on visual-textual alignment quality.

\textbf{Prescribed Transition Match (PTM)---microscopic transition fidelity.}
\label{sec:ptm_def}
PTM restricts evaluation to the \emph{prescribed subspace}---the $\Delta S^* \neq 0$ cells where narrative-defining transitions occur.
Let $\mathcal{M} \in \{0,1\}^{(T-1)\times N}$ be the \emph{prescription mask} with $\mathcal{M}_{t,e} = \mathbb{1}[\Delta S^*_{t,e} \neq 0]$, and define $\mathcal{A} = \{(t,e) : \mathcal{M}_{t,e} = 1\}$.
Then:
\begin{equation}
\text{PTM} = 1 - \frac{\|E \odot \mathcal{M}\|_0}{|\mathcal{A}|},
\label{eq:ptm}
\end{equation}
where $\odot$ denotes the Hadamard product and $\|\cdot\|_0$ counts non-zero entries.
Equivalently, $\text{PTM} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{A}|} \sum_{(t,e) \in \mathcal{A}} \mathbb{1}[E_{t,e} = 0]$.
By masking out hold cells ($\Delta S^* = 0$), PTM eliminates the dominant VLM noise source while retaining the entries that distinguish narrative success from failure.

\textbf{Entity Presence Accuracy (EPA)---macroscopic cast compliance.}
\label{sec:epa_def}
EPA operates on the presence matrix $S$ rather than its derivative $\Delta S$, providing a macroscopic view of static cast compliance.
Let $S_\text{err} = S_\text{obs} - S^*$ denote the presence error matrix:
\begin{equation}
\text{EPA} = 1 - \frac{\|S_\text{err}\|_0}{T \cdot N}.
\label{eq:epa}
\end{equation}
Equivalently, $\text{EPA} = \frac{1}{TN}\sum_{t,k} \mathbb{1}[S_{\text{obs},\,tk} = S^*_{tk}]$.
While PTM evaluates whether the \emph{dynamics} are correct (did the right transitions happen?), EPA evaluates whether the \emph{states} are correct (are the right entities present in each shot?).
The $\ell_0$-norm formulation makes explicit that both metrics count discrete mismatches in complementary matrix subspaces---prescribed transitions for PTM, full presence for EPA---unifying them under a single algebraic framework.

\section{The MEMS-1K Benchmark}
\label{sec:dataset}
%% ============================================================

We construct \textbf{MEMS-1K} (\textbf{M}ulti-\textbf{E}ntity \textbf{M}ulti-\textbf{S}hot \textbf{1K}), a dataset of 1{,}000 multi-entity scenarios (3{,}000 shots total) spanning all 7 narrative patterns.
Basis patterns receive 200 prompts each. Accumulation, although algebraically derived, introduces a third entity and therefore also receives 200 prompts to ensure empirical coverage of 3-entity dynamics.
The remaining derived patterns receive 100 prompts each (Table~\ref{tab:pattern_dist}), reflecting the algebraic structure where they are compositions of basis operations.

\textbf{Finding 2: Consistent rank reversal against IC.}

\subsection{Metric Sensitivity Across Patterns}
\label{sec:pattern_analysis}

The per-pattern breakdown in Table~\ref{tab:main} reveals how \stem{} metrics expose architectural limitations invisible to aggregate scores.

\textbf{Pattern difficulty gradient.}
PTM spread (best minus worst) varies from 0.11 (Relay, Reverse Relay) to 0.28 (Sliding Window), confirming that more complex patterns produce stronger metric discrimination.
The 4-transition patterns (Convergence, Sliding Window) consistently separate all four methods, while 2-transition patterns (Relay) show smaller inter-method gaps.

\textbf{Architectural patterns emerge.}
StoryDiffusion's Consistent Self-Attention excels at Sliding Window (0.357) and Convergence (0.277), the patterns requiring complex multi-entity transitions.
VGoT's cascaded pipeline collapses on Convergence (PTM = 0.053, the lowest overall value), revealing that DynamiCrafter cannot render new entities on demand.
VIC shows near-uniform failure (PTM range = 0.032), confirming that in-context conditioning creates a fixed coherence ceiling invariant to pattern complexity.

The error taxonomy provides actionable diagnostics that no scalar metric can offer (Table~\ref{tab:failures}, Fig.~\ref{fig:qualitative}).
\textbf{Context Bleeding} (Type~I) is worst for VIC (75.6\%): its in-context conditioning explicitly references prior shots, making entity removal the hardest operation.
\textbf{Amnesia} (Type~II) is worst for VGoT (82.1\%): a planning-rendering bottleneck where DynamiCrafter cannot introduce new entities absent from the keyframe.
\textbf{Spurious transitions} (Type~III) are most frequent for StoryDiffusion (51.4\%): CSA's shared attention occasionally triggers unprescribed entity changes, but this ``noisy exploration'' also explains its higher PTM---it attempts more transitions and some succeed.
\textbf{Reversals} (Type~IV, $|E_{t,e}| = 2$) are rare but the most severe (7.6--13.9\%), representing cases where the observed transition is \emph{diametrically opposite} to prescribed---e.g., an entity enters when it should exit. Type~IV is a strict subset of Types~I/II, indicating compounded failure where both presence and transition are wrong.
Fewer than 1.5\% of videos are failure-free even for the best model, confirming that multi-entity narrative execution remains an unsolved challenge across all generation paradigms.

The four models lie on a negative slope ($\rho_s = -1.0$, $n\!=\!4$), with VIC at the high-IC/low-PTM corner and StoryDiffusion at the opposite end.

\subsection{Human Evaluation}
\label{sec:human_eval}

To validate automated metrics against human perception, 15 evaluators (graduate students in computer science with video generation experience) assess 40 randomly sampled scenarios.
Each scenario is shown as a pairwise comparison of two models' outputs alongside the original per-shot text prompts.
Evaluators rate each pair on 5-point Likert scales for \emph{Narrative Compliance} (``Which video better follows the prescribed entity dynamics?'').
Each comparison is rated by 3 evaluators (120 total comparisons).

PTM rankings match human narrative judgments ($\rho = 0.80$, $p < 0.05$; Fleiss' $\kappa = 0.67$), validating it as a reliable automated proxy.
VIC ranks last in human narrative preference (17.6\% win rate).
The high narrative tie rate (42.3\%) independently confirms the pervasiveness of narrative failures---ties predominantly reflect mutual failure where both models fail to execute prescribed transitions.


\subsection{Discussion}

\paragraph{VLM detection limitations.}
The VLM achieves 52\% TPR and 67\% TNR---imperfect, but \emph{systematically} imperfect across all methods.
The key property is \emph{discriminative validity}, not absolute accuracy.
PTM achieves this with $H = 302.7$ ($p < 10^{-65}$), non-overlapping CIs, and effect sizes up to $d = 0.72$.
The 3.5$\times$ gap between SD and VIC is far larger than CLIP-based metrics ($<$0.06 gap).
We also evaluated Grounding DINO~\cite{groundingdino} (TNR $= 4\%$) and smaller VLMs (2B: TPR $= 40\%$), confirming 7B VLM as the best option (Appendix).

\paragraph{Why decompose $E$?}
A natural first attempt is to use the raw matrix distance $\|E\|_F$ as a single metric.
However, in a typical $2 \times 3$ transition matrix, hold cells ($\Delta S^* = 0$) outnumber prescribed cells by 2--4$\times$, so VLM noise in hold cells dominates $\|E\|_F$ and drowns the narrative signal ($\|E\|_F$ achieves only 1 of 6 pairwise significances at $\alpha = 0.05$; Appendix).
The $\ell_0$-norm decomposition into PTM (masked to prescribed cells) and EPA (operating on presence states) is the principled solution: it separates narrative-relevant signal from detector noise, enabling the non-overlapping CIs and $H = 302.7$ reported above.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ============================================================

We presented the \diagonal{} framework, which formalizes multi-entity, multi-shot narrative evaluation as matrix matching.
Theorem~1 proves that two basis matrices (Relay and Split) span the $T\!=\!3$, $N\!\leq\!3$ transition space, with Theorem~2 extending the result to arbitrary $T$ and $N$.
The \stem{} evaluation reduces narrative assessment to the error matrix $E = \Delta S_\text{obs} - \Delta S^*$, decomposed into PTM (transition fidelity) and EPA (cast compliance).
On MEMS-1K, PTM discriminates all four architectures with non-overlapping 95\% confidence intervals, a Kruskal--Wallis statistic of $H = 302.7$ ($p < 10^{-65}$), and a 3.5$\times$ gap between StoryDiffusion (0.243) and VIC (0.070).
The four-type failure taxonomy provides the first structured diagnostic of multi-entity narrative failures, revealing that fewer than 1.5\% of videos are error-free across all models.

\paragraph{Future directions.}
Our results suggest that entity-aware conditioning mechanisms---entity-specific attention masks~\cite{flexattention}, grounded generation~\cite{gligen}, or per-entity guidance scales---may be needed to bridge the gap between visual coherence and narrative compliance.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
\diagonal{} evaluates 3-shot, $\leq$3-entity sequences. Theorem~2 (Appendix~B) proves formal generalization to $T$ shots and $N$ entities.
The VLM detector (52\% TPR, 67\% TNR) introduces systematic noise. While discriminative validity is established, stronger VLMs would improve absolute accuracy.
We release all prompts, evaluation code, and 4{,}000 generated videos for reproducibility.
