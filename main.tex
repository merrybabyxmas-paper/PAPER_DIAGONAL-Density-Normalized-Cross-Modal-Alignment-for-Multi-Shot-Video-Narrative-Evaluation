\begin{abstract}
Multi-shot video generation models can now produce visually coherent sequences, yet they face a fundamental \emph{coherence--compliance trade-off}: the very mechanisms that maintain visual consistency across shots---shared attention, in-context conditioning---prevent models from executing the dynamic entity entrances and exits that define storytelling.
Current evaluation paradigms, heavily optimized for visual quality, systematically overlook this tension.
We present the \diagonal{} framework, which formalizes multi-entity, multi-shot narratives as \emph{state transition matrices} $\Delta S \in \{-1,0,+1\}^{(T-1) \times N}$ and proves that \textbf{every conceivable narrative topology} can be generated from just two algebraic basis matrices (Relay and Split) under column permutation and integer linear combination (\textbf{Theorem~1}).
This elevates video evaluation from heuristic prompting to \emph{mathematically complete formal verification}.
To evaluate generated videos, we introduce \stem{} (State Transition Error Matching): the error matrix $E = \Delta S_\text{obs} - \Delta S^*$ captures all narrative failures in a single algebraic object, decomposed via an $\ell_0$-norm formulation into \emph{Prescribed Transition Match} (PTM) for microscopic transition fidelity and \emph{Entity Presence Accuracy} (EPA) for macroscopic cast compliance.
On MEMS-1K (1{,}000 scenarios, 7 patterns, 4{,}000 videos from four architectures), PTM produces non-overlapping confidence intervals ($H = 302.7$, $p < 10^{-65}$), a 3.5$\times$ gap between best and worst models, and a consistent rank reversal against Identity Consistency---exposing a previously invisible architectural tension.
A four-type failure taxonomy derived from $E$ provides structured, actionable diagnostics that complement existing scalar metrics.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}
%% ============================================================

Multi-shot video generation models now produce visually compelling sequences~\cite{sora,storydiff,moviegen,ho2022video,svd,imagenvideo}, yet they face a fundamental \emph{coherence--compliance trade-off}: the very mechanisms that enforce cross-shot visual consistency---shared attention, in-context conditioning---suppress the localized entity control needed to execute dynamic entrances and exits.
Existing benchmarks~\cite{vbench,vbench2,t2vcompbench}, optimized for single-shot visual quality, collapse multi-entity dynamics into frame-level similarity scores where two characters meeting and then parting receives the same rating as their remaining together throughout.

The root problem is combinatorial.
For $T$ shots and $N$ entities, the presence matrix $S \in \{0,1\}^{T \times N}$ admits $2^{TN}$ configurations.
No existing benchmark provides formal coverage guarantees over this space; prior benchmarks~\cite{moviebench,locot2v,shotadapter} rely on empirically curated prompt sets without mathematical assurance that the evaluated scenarios span the full narrative space.
We reformulate the problem in linear algebra: a narrative script becomes a \emph{state transition matrix} $\Delta S \in \{-1,0,+1\}^{(T-1)\times N}$, and we prove that \textbf{every conceivable narrative topology} for $T\!=\!3$, $N\!\leq\!3$ can be generated from just \textbf{two algebraic basis matrices} (Relay and Split) under column permutation and integer linear combination (\textbf{Theorem~1}).
This is the first result that elevates video evaluation from heuristic scenario selection to \emph{mathematically complete formal verification}: testing a model on our basis-derived patterns certifies its behavior over the \emph{entire} narrative space.

We contribute:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
  \item The \textbf{\diagonal{} framework} (\S\ref{sec:framework}): state transition matrices for $N$-entity, $M$-shot narratives, with a proof that all matrices are spanned by just two basis patterns---Relay and Split (\textbf{Theorem~1}, \S\ref{sec:completeness}). \textbf{Theorem~2} (Appendix~B) extends the result to arbitrary $T$ and $N$.
  \item The \textbf{\stem{} evaluation pipeline} (\S\ref{sec:stem}): the error matrix $E = \Delta S_\text{obs} - \Delta S^*$ as the single evaluation object, decomposed via $\ell_0$-norm into microscopic PTM and macroscopic EPA.
  \item \textbf{MEMS-1K} (\S\ref{sec:dataset}), a benchmark of 1{,}000 multi-entity, multi-shot scenarios with 7 narrative patterns, a \textbf{four-type failure taxonomy}, and 4{,}000 videos across four architectures.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
%% ============================================================

\paragraph{Text-to-video generation.}
Modern video synthesis rests on diffusion models~\cite{ddpm,ldm,scoreflow}, extended temporally through UNet attention~\cite{svd,imagenvideo,ho2022video}, scalable DiT architectures~\cite{dit,wan,sora} with transformer backbones~\cite{attention,vit}, flow-matching~\cite{flowmatching}, and classifier-free guidance~\cite{cfg}.
Open-source systems such as Open-Sora~\cite{opensora} and AnimateDiff~\cite{animatediff} provide general-purpose motion modules, while Lumiere~\cite{lumiere} and Emu Video~\cite{emuvideo} explore space-time generation, but multi-entity narrative control remains an open problem.

\paragraph{Multi-shot video generation.}
Multi-shot extensions address cross-shot coherence via Consistent Self-Attention (CSA)~\cite{storydiff,consistory}, cross-attention~\cite{videostudio}, latent initialization~\cite{mevg}, LLM-guided layouts~\cite{vgot,videodirectorgpt,storygen}, keyframe transitions~\cite{seine,dynamicrafter}, flow-matching portraits~\cite{echoshot}, IP-Adapter~\cite{ip_adapter}, in-context LoRA~\cite{vic,lora}, subject customization~\cite{dreamvideo,tuneavideo}, and multimodal storytelling~\cite{minidalle,moviellm}.
Longer-horizon generation has been explored through autoregressive token models~\cite{videopoet,phenaki} and visual pre-training~\cite{nuwa}, though these operate at the single-clip level.
We evaluate representatives from four paradigms---CSA (StoryDiffusion), flow-matching (EchoShot), in-context DiT (VIC), and cascaded T2I+I2V (VGoT)---providing the first cross-paradigm comparison on narrative dynamics.

\paragraph{Video generation benchmarks.}
Existing benchmarks quantify consistency via embedding similarity (Table~\ref{tab:benchmark}).
VBench~\cite{vbench,vbench2} computes frame-level CLIP~\cite{clip}/DINO~\cite{dinov2} similarities that conflate subject and background.
T2V-CompBench~\cite{t2vcompbench} targets single-clip compositionality.
ShotAdapter~\cite{shotadapter} isolates subject regions but cannot decompose multi-entity presence at the shot level.
MovieBench~\cite{moviebench} and LoCoT2V~\cite{locot2v} recognize multi-shot challenges but lack entity-level metrics and mathematical formalization.
FID~\cite{fid} and FVD~\cite{fvd} measure distributional fidelity but are agnostic to narrative structure.
CLIPScore~\cite{clipscore} measures text--image alignment at the single-frame level only.
These benchmarks rely on empirically curated prompt sets without formal coverage guarantees over the combinatorial narrative space.
\diagonal{} complements this line of work by formalizing multi-entity, multi-shot narratives via state transition matrices, with a mathematical proof that its basis patterns span the \emph{entire} narrative space.

\section{The \diagonal{} Framework}
\label{sec:framework}
%% ============================================================

We first build the theoretical foundation: state transition matrices (\S\ref{sec:formalization}), their algebraic basis (\S\ref{sec:basis}), and the completeness proof (\S\ref{sec:completeness}).
Section~\ref{sec:stem} then develops the practical evaluation pipeline.

\subsection{Narrative as State Transition Matrices}
\label{sec:formalization}

A multi-shot video consists of $T$ shots depicting up to $N$ named entities (e.g., ``a chef,'' ``a dog'').

\paragraph{Presence matrix.}
The binary matrix $S \in \{0,1\}^{T \times N}$ records entity visibility:
\begin{equation}
S_{t,e} =
\begin{cases}
  1 & \text{if entity } e \text{ appears in shot } t, \\
  0 & \text{otherwise.}
\end{cases}
\label{eq:presence}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{State transition matrix.}
Temporal differencing extracts dynamics:
\begin{equation}
\Delta S \;=\; S_{2:T} \;-\; S_{1:T\!-\!1},
\qquad \Delta S_{t,e} \in \{-1,\; 0,\; +1\},
\label{eq:delta_s}
\end{equation}
where $+1$ = \emph{enter}, $-1$ = \emph{exit}, $0$ = \emph{hold}.

\paragraph{Example: Relay ($A \!\to\! AB \!\to\! B$).}
\begin{equation}
S = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix},
\qquad
\Delta S = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & +1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}.
\label{eq:relay_example}
\end{equation}
Entity $B$ enters at transition~1; entity $A$ exits at transition~2.

\paragraph{Example: Accumulation ($A \!\to\! AB \!\to\! ABC$).}
\begin{equation}
S = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix},
\qquad
\Delta S = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & +1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & +1 \end{bmatrix}.
\label{eq:accum_example}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Canonical form.}
For $T\!=\!3$ shots, $\Delta S$ has 2 rows; for $N$ entities, $N$ columns.
We zero-pad all matrices to $\{-1,0,+1\}^{2 \times 3}$ so that 2-entity patterns embed naturally:
\begin{equation}
\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} 0 & +1 \\ -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}}_{\text{Relay}~(2\!\times\!2)}
\;\;\longrightarrow\;\;
\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} 0 & +1 & 0 \\ -1 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}}_{\text{canonical}~(2\!\times\!3)}.
\label{eq:canonical}
\end{equation}
This enables a unified proof over $\mathbb{M}_{2 \times 3}(\mathbb{Z})$.
The extension to arbitrary $T$ and $N$ is given in Appendix~B (Theorem~2).

\subsection{Linear Operators on Narrative Matrices}
\label{sec:basis}

We define two operators that manipulate the narrative topology.

\begin{definition}[Column Permutation]
\label{def:col_perm}
Let $\sigma_{ij}$ denote the transposition swapping columns $i$ and $j$.
The column permutation operator reassigns entity roles without altering the causal topology:
\begin{equation}
(\sigma_{ij} \circ \Delta S)_{t,e} = \Delta S_{t,\,\sigma_{ij}^{-1}(e)}.
\label{eq:col_perm}
\end{equation}
Compositions read left-to-right: $\sigma_{23} \circ \sigma_{12}$ first swaps columns 1--2, then 2--3.
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Time Reversal]
\label{def:time_rev}
The time reversal operator $\calT$ inverts the temporal order and negates all signs, converting entries to exits and vice versa:
\begin{equation}
(\calT \circ \Delta S)_{t,e} = -\Delta S_{(T\!-\!1)-t,\,e}.
\label{eq:time_rev}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Basis Matrices]
\label{def:basis}
Two matrices form the algebraic basis of the narrative space (all in canonical $2 \times 3$ form):

\emph{Relay} ($\calB_1$): a sequential hand-off ($A \!\to\! AB \!\to\! B$):
\begin{equation}
\calB_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & +1 & 0 \\ -1 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}.
\label{eq:basis_relay}
\end{equation}

\emph{Split} ($\calB_2$): co-occurring entities separate ($AB \!\to\! A \!\to\! B$):
\begin{equation}
\calB_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 & 0 \\ -1 & +1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}.
\label{eq:basis_split}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

Five additional patterns are constructed from $\calB_1$, $\calB_2$ via $\sigma_{ij}$, $\calT$, and $\mathbb{Z}$-linear combination:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
  \item \textbf{Reverse Relay} ($B \!\to\! AB \!\to\! A$): $\sigma_{12} \circ \calB_1 = \calT \circ \calB_1 = \left[\begin{smallmatrix} +1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 \end{smallmatrix}\right]$.
  \item \textbf{Convergence} ($A \!\to\! B \!\to\! AB$): $\calT \circ (\sigma_{12} \circ \calB_2) = \left[\begin{smallmatrix} -1 & +1 & 0 \\ +1 & 0 & 0 \end{smallmatrix}\right]$.
  \item \textbf{Accumulation} ($A \!\to\! AB \!\to\! ABC$): $E_{12} + E_{23} = \left[\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & +1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & +1 \end{smallmatrix}\right]$, where $E_{ij} \in \operatorname{span}_{\mathbb{Z}}(\calB_1, \calB_2, \Sigma_3)$ by Theorem~\ref{thm:completeness}. Introduces a third entity.
  \item \textbf{Reduction} ($ABC \!\to\! AB \!\to\! A$): $\calT \circ (\text{Accumulation}) = \left[\begin{smallmatrix} 0 & 0 & -1 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 \end{smallmatrix}\right]$.
  \item \textbf{Sliding Window} ($AB \!\to\! BC \!\to\! C$): $-E_{11} + E_{13} - E_{22} = \left[\begin{smallmatrix} -1 & 0 & +1 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 \end{smallmatrix}\right]$, involving all 3 entities across both transitions.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Completeness Theorem}
\label{sec:completeness}

\begin{theorem}[Completeness of the Narrative Space]
\label{thm:completeness}
Under column permutation $\sigma \in \Sigma_3$ and integer-coefficient linear combination, the two basis patterns $\calB_1$ (Relay) and $\calB_2$ (Split) generate all matrices in $\mathbb{M}_{2 \times 3}(\mathbb{Z})$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
It suffices to construct all six standard basis matrices $E_{ij}$ (1 in position $(i,j)$, 0 elsewhere) from $\calB_1$ and $\calB_2$, since any integer matrix is a $\mathbb{Z}$-linear combination of $\{E_{ij}\}$.

\emph{Step 1: Isolate $E_{11} - E_{21}$.}\;
We compute two permutations of $\calB_1$:
\begin{equation}
\sigma_{13} \circ \calB_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & +1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}, \quad
\sigma_{23} \circ \sigma_{12} \circ \calB_1 = \begin{bmatrix} +1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}.
\label{eq:two_perms}
\end{equation}
The linear combination $\calB_1 - (\sigma_{13} \circ \calB_1) + (\sigma_{23} \circ \sigma_{12} \circ \calB_1)$ yields:
\begin{align}
&\begin{bmatrix} 0 & +1 & 0 \\ -1 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}
- \begin{bmatrix} 0 & +1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}
+ \begin{bmatrix} +1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \notag \\[3pt]
&\;= \begin{bmatrix} +1 & 0 & 0 \\ -1 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}
\;= E_{11} - E_{21}. \label{eq:e11_e21}
\end{align}

\emph{Step 2: Extract the standard basis $\{E_{ij}\}$.}\;
Adding $\sigma_{12} \circ \calB_2 = \left[\begin{smallmatrix} -1 & 0 & 0 \\ +1 & -1 & 0 \end{smallmatrix}\right]$ to Eq.~\ref{eq:e11_e21} isolates $-E_{22}$:
\begin{align}
(E_{11} - E_{21}) + \sigma_{12} \circ \calB_2
&= \begin{bmatrix} +1 & 0 & 0 \\ -1 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}
 + \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 & 0 \\ +1 & -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \notag \\[3pt]
&= \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} = -E_{22}. \label{eq:isolate_e22}
\end{align}
From $E_{22}$, column permutations yield $E_{21} = \sigma_{12} \circ E_{22}$ and $E_{23} = \sigma_{23} \circ E_{22}$.
\emph{Adding} $E_{21}$ to Eq.~\ref{eq:e11_e21}: $(E_{11} - E_{21}) + E_{21} = E_{11}$.
Column permutations of $E_{11}$ produce $E_{12} = \sigma_{12} \circ E_{11}$ and $E_{13} = \sigma_{13} \circ E_{11}$:
\begin{equation}
\{E_{11},\, E_{12},\, E_{13},\, E_{21},\, E_{22},\, E_{23}\} \;\subset\; \operatorname{span}_{\mathbb{Z}}\!\bigl(\calB_1,\, \calB_2,\, \Sigma_3\bigr). \qedhere
\label{eq:all_eij}
\end{equation}
\end{proof}

\noindent The complete derivation with all intermediate matrices is provided in Appendix~A.
Note that all permutations of $\calB_1$ share the row-sum constraint $(+1, -1)$, so $\calB_2$ is essential to break this symmetry---no subset of $\calB_1$ permutations alone can span $\mathbb{M}_{2 \times 3}(\mathbb{Z})$.

\paragraph{Implications.}

\emph{Completeness.}\;
\diagonal{}'s seven patterns form a \emph{mathematically complete} evaluation suite:
\begin{equation}
\forall\, M \in \mathbb{M}_{2 \times 3}(\mathbb{Z}),\quad
M = \sum_{i,j} \alpha_{ij}\, E_{ij}, \quad \alpha_{ij} \in \mathbb{Z},
\label{eq:completeness_expansion}
\end{equation}
so testing a model on these patterns certifies its behavior on \emph{any} narrative topology, including patterns not explicitly in the benchmark.
This is a key distinction from prior benchmarks~\cite{moviebench,locot2v,shotadapter}, which rely on empirically curated prompt sets without formal coverage guarantees.

\emph{Scalability.}\;
For $T$-shot, $N$-entity narratives, the target space is $\mathbb{M}_{(T-1) \times N}(\mathbb{Z})$, which has dimension:
\begin{equation}
\dim = 2\,(T\!-\!1)\,N,
\label{eq:scaling}
\end{equation}
achievable with $2(T\!-\!1)$ basis matrices via spatial zero-padding and temporal shift operators---linear, not combinatorial, growth (Theorem~2, Appendix~B).

\section{The \stem{} Evaluation Methodology}
\label{sec:stem}
%% ============================================================

Building upon the theoretical completeness of the \diagonal{} framework, we introduce \stem{} (State Transition Error Matching), a fully automated evaluation pipeline.
\stem{} bridges the gap between the continuous visual space and our discrete algebraic framework by employing Vision-Language Models (VLMs) as state detectors, ultimately yielding density-normalized $\ell_0$-norm metrics.

\subsection{VLM-Based State Detection}
\label{sec:vlm_instantiation}

To evaluate a generated video, we must first map it to the observed presence matrix $S_\text{obs} \in \{0,1\}^{T \times N}$.
We extract the middle frame of each shot $t \in \{1, \ldots, T\}$ and query a VLM with a binary question for each prescribed entity $e$:
\begin{equation}
S_\text{obs}[t, e] = \mathbb{1}\!\bigl[\text{VLM}(\text{frame}_t,\;\text{``Is entity } e \text{ visible?''}) = \text{Yes}\bigr].
\label{eq:vlm_probe}
\end{equation}
We utilize Qwen2-VL-7B-Instruct~\cite{qwen2vl} for this detection.
Formulating detection as discrete binary queries circumvents the limitations of continuous contrastive probes (e.g., CLIP similarity), which suffer from bag-of-words effects and marginal present--absent score gaps (Cohen's $d = 0.23$--$0.59$), often requiring fragile, dataset-specific threshold tuning.

\subsection{Algebraic Error Matrix and Failure Taxonomy}
\label{sec:error_taxonomy}

Given $S_\text{obs}$, we compute the observed state transition matrix $\Delta S_\text{obs} = S_{\text{obs},\,2:T} - S_{\text{obs},\,1:T\!-\!1}$.
The core of our evaluation is the \emph{error matrix}, which captures all narrative deviations in a single algebraic object.

\begin{definition}[Error Matrix]
\label{def:error_matrix}
Let $\Delta S^*$ be the prescribed transition matrix.
The error matrix $E \in \{-2,\ldots,+2\}^{(T-1) \times N}$ is:
\begin{equation}
E \;=\; \Delta S_\text{obs} \;-\; \Delta S^*.
\label{eq:error_matrix}
\end{equation}
A perfect narrative execution yields $E = \mathbf{0}$.
Any non-zero entry uniquely localizes a failure to a specific transition--entity pair $(t, e)$.
\end{definition}

The algebraic structure of $E$ naturally induces an exhaustive taxonomy of narrative failures (Fig.~\ref{fig:overview}(c)).

\begin{definition}[Failure Taxonomy]
\label{def:failure_taxonomy}
Based on the joint state of $(E_{t,e},\, \Delta S^*_{t,e})$, we classify transition failures into four types:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,nosep]
  \item \textbf{Type~I} (Context Bleeding): $\Delta S^*_{t,e} = -1$ and $E_{t,e} > 0$. An entity persists when it should have exited.
  \item \textbf{Type~II} (Amnesia): $\Delta S^*_{t,e} = +1$ and $E_{t,e} < 0$. An entity fails to appear at its prescribed entry.
  \item \textbf{Type~III} (Spurious): $\Delta S^*_{t,e} = 0$ and $E_{t,e} \neq 0$. An unprescribed change occurs in a hold cell.
  \item \textbf{Type~IV} (Reversal): $|E_{t,e}| = 2$. The observed transition is diametrically opposite to the prescription.
\end{itemize}
\end{definition}

\noindent\emph{Note.}\;
Type~IV represents the most severe compounded failure and is algebraically a strict subset of Types~I/II: a single $|E_{t,e}| = 2$ entry simultaneously satisfies the condition for its parent type and for Type~IV.
The failure counts in Table~\ref{tab:failures} report each type independently.

\subsection{Density-Normalized $\ell_0$-Norm Metrics}
\label{sec:metrics}

A naive approach to quantifying $E$ would be to compute the Frobenius norm $\lVert E \rVert_F$.
However, in a standard narrative matrix, hold cells ($\Delta S^*_{t,e} = 0$) outnumber active transitions by 2--4$\times$.
Consequently, VLM detection noise in hold cells dominates the unnormalized distance, drowning the true narrative signal ($\lVert E \rVert_F$ achieves only 1 of 6 pairwise significances at $\alpha = 0.05$; Appendix).
To rigorously isolate narrative fidelity, we decompose the evaluation into two complementary $\ell_0$-norm metrics.

\begin{definition}[Prescribed Transition Match (PTM)]
\label{def:ptm}
PTM quantifies \emph{microscopic transition fidelity} by evaluating only the prescribed dynamic changes.
Let $\mathcal{M} \in \{0,1\}^{(T-1) \times N}$ be the binary prescription mask with $\mathcal{M}_{t,e} = \mathbb{1}[\Delta S^*_{t,e} \neq 0]$.
Then:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;\text{PTM} = 1 - \frac{\lVert E \odot \mathcal{M} \rVert_0}{\lVert \mathcal{M} \rVert_0}\;}
\label{eq:ptm}
\end{equation}
where $\odot$ denotes the Hadamard product, $\lVert \cdot \rVert_0$ counts non-zero entries, and $\lVert \mathcal{M} \rVert_0$ is the total number of prescribed transitions.
\end{definition}

\noindent By masking out hold cells, PTM elegantly eliminates the dominant source of VLM noise while capturing the exact operations that define storytelling.

\begin{definition}[Entity Presence Accuracy (EPA)]
\label{def:epa}
EPA assesses \emph{macroscopic cast compliance} by computing the element-wise accuracy of the presence states:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\;\text{EPA} = 1 - \frac{\lVert S_\text{obs} - S^* \rVert_0}{T \cdot N}\;}
\label{eq:epa}
\end{equation}
\end{definition}

\noindent Together, PTM (``Did the right \emph{transitions} happen?'') and EPA (``Are the right \emph{entities} present?'') provide a complete, density-normalized evaluation of multi-shot video generation---counting discrete mismatches via $\ell_0$-norms in complementary matrix subspaces.

\subsection{Metric Sensitivity Across Patterns}
\label{sec:pattern_analysis}

The per-pattern breakdown in Table~\ref{tab:main} reveals how \stem{} metrics expose architectural limitations invisible to aggregate scores.

\textbf{Pattern difficulty gradient.}
PTM spread (best minus worst) varies from 0.11 (Relay, Reverse Relay) to 0.28 (Sliding Window), confirming that more complex patterns produce stronger metric discrimination.
The 3-transition patterns (Split, Convergence, Sliding Window) consistently separate all four methods, while 2-transition patterns (Relay) show smaller inter-method gaps.

\textbf{Architectural patterns emerge.}
StoryDiffusion's Consistent Self-Attention excels at Sliding Window (0.357) and Convergence (0.277), the patterns requiring complex multi-entity transitions.
VGoT's cascaded pipeline collapses on Convergence (PTM = 0.053, the lowest overall value), revealing that DynamiCrafter cannot render new entities on demand.
VIC shows near-uniform failure (PTM range = 0.032), confirming that in-context conditioning creates a fixed coherence ceiling invariant to pattern complexity.

\subsection{Human Evaluation}
\label{sec:human_eval}

To validate automated metrics against human perception, 15 evaluators (graduate students in computer science with video generation experience) assess 40 randomly sampled scenarios.
Each scenario is shown as a pairwise comparison of two models' outputs alongside the original per-shot text prompts.
Evaluators rate each pair on 5-point Likert scales for \emph{Narrative Compliance} (``Which video better follows the prescribed entity dynamics?'').
Each comparison is rated by 3 evaluators (120 total comparisons).

PTM rankings match human narrative judgments ($\rho = 0.80$, $p < 0.05$; Fleiss' $\kappa = 0.67$), validating it as a reliable automated proxy.
VIC ranks last in human narrative preference (17.6\% win rate).
The high narrative tie rate (42.3\%) independently confirms the pervasiveness of narrative failures---ties predominantly reflect mutual failure where both models fail to execute prescribed transitions.


\subsection{Discussion}

\paragraph{VLM detection limitations.}
The VLM achieves 52\% TPR and 67\% TNR---imperfect, but \emph{systematically} imperfect across all methods.
The key property is \emph{discriminative validity}, not absolute accuracy.
PTM achieves this with $H = 302.7$ ($p < 10^{-65}$), non-overlapping CIs, and effect sizes up to $d = 0.72$.
The 3.5$\times$ gap between SD and VIC is far larger than CLIP-based metrics ($<$0.06 gap).
We also evaluated Grounding DINO~\cite{groundingdino} (TNR $= 4\%$) and smaller VLMs (2B: TPR $= 40\%$), confirming 7B VLM as the best option (Appendix).

\paragraph{Why decompose $E$?}
A natural first attempt is to use the raw matrix distance $\|E\|_F$ as a single metric.
However, in a typical $2 \times 3$ transition matrix, hold cells ($\Delta S^* = 0$) outnumber prescribed cells by 2--4$\times$, so VLM noise in hold cells dominates $\|E\|_F$ and drowns the narrative signal ($\|E\|_F$ achieves only 1 of 6 pairwise significances at $\alpha = 0.05$; Appendix).
The $\ell_0$-norm decomposition into PTM (masked to prescribed cells) and EPA (operating on presence states) is the principled solution: it separates narrative-relevant signal from detector noise, enabling the non-overlapping CIs and $H = 302.7$ reported above.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ============================================================

We presented the \diagonal{} framework, which formalizes multi-entity, multi-shot narrative evaluation as matrix matching.
Theorem~1 proves that two basis matrices (Relay and Split) span the $T\!=\!3$, $N\!\leq\!3$ transition space, with Theorem~2 extending the result to arbitrary $T$ and $N$.
The \stem{} evaluation reduces narrative assessment to the error matrix $E = \Delta S_\text{obs} - \Delta S^*$, decomposed into PTM (transition fidelity) and EPA (cast compliance).
On MEMS-1K, PTM discriminates all four architectures with non-overlapping 95\% confidence intervals, a Kruskal--Wallis statistic of $H = 302.7$ ($p < 10^{-65}$), and a 3.5$\times$ gap between StoryDiffusion (0.243) and VIC (0.070).
The four-type failure taxonomy provides the first structured diagnostic of multi-entity narrative failures, revealing that fewer than 1.5\% of videos are error-free across all models.

\paragraph{Future directions.}
Our results suggest that entity-aware conditioning mechanisms---entity-specific attention masks~\cite{flexattention}, grounded generation~\cite{gligen}, or per-entity guidance scales---may be needed to bridge the gap between visual coherence and narrative compliance.

\paragraph{Limitations.}
\diagonal{} evaluates 3-shot, $\leq$3-entity sequences. Theorem~2 (Appendix~B) proves formal generalization to $T$ shots and $N$ entities.
The VLM detector (52\% TPR, 67\% TNR) introduces systematic noise. While discriminative validity is established, stronger VLMs would improve absolute accuracy.
We release all prompts, evaluation code, and 4{,}000 generated videos for reproducibility.
